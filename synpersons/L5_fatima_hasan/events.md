## E001 · 2009-07 → 2010-06 · intensity: 8
**Manchester LLB, the First Real Client**

I graduated and started my training contract at a mid-size Manchester firm. My first immigration file — a Congolese asylum seeker whose claim had been refused on procedural grounds that I thought were incorrectly applied — was the case that fixed the direction. I researched the procedural question for three days, drafted a skeleton argument, showed it to my supervising solicitor, and she filed it under her name because I was a trainee. The claim was allowed. I never met the client. I understood that the rule, correctly applied, had produced the right outcome, and that knowing this without meeting the person whose life it changed was the specific condition of this work.

## E002 · 2011-04 → 2012-01 · intensity: 7
**The Hostile Environment Escalation**

The UK Home Office's hostile environment policy began escalating in 2011. The practical effects in Manchester were immediate and specific: landlord checks, bank account restrictions, increased refusal rates on applications that had been approved on similar facts two years before. I documented the refusal patterns in a spreadsheet I kept on my personal laptop, not because anyone asked me to but because the pattern deserved documentation. My senior colleague said I was building a picture that wouldn't help my current clients. I said: it might help the next ones. He said: that's not billable time. I said I knew.

## E003 · 2014-07 → 2015-04 · intensity: 7
**Meeting Tariq**

Tariq covered an immigration tribunal case I'd argued for a Guardian piece on the hostile environment policy. He asked me for a comment I gave him precisely and he printed it precisely, which I noted because journalists frequently don't. We had coffee to discuss the policy context further and the coffee lasted three hours. He said: you're more interesting than most of the lawyers I talk to because you separate what the law says from what you think the law should say. I said: those are different professional obligations. He said: I know. That's interesting. We were engaged two years later. The professional tension between his work and mine has been present since that first conversation and productive since.

## E004 · 2016-10 → 2017-07 · intensity: 7
**Opening Hasan & Partners**

I registered the firm in October 2016. The timing was deliberate: the hostile environment policy had expanded, the major Manchester firms were deprioritizing legally aided immigration work because of fee cut, and there was a structural gap between what clients needed and what the market was providing. I had £40,000 in savings, a serviced office in the Northern Quarter, and Miriam Gold's word that she'd refer cases she couldn't take. The first year I worked sixteen-hour days six days a week. My father asked if this was sustainable. I said: not forever. He said: then make sure forever comes before you do.

## E005 · 2018-03 → 2018-11 · intensity: 6
**The Windrush Scandal**

The Windrush scandal broke in March 2018. I had four clients in the same situation — long-term settled residents being treated as illegal immigrants because their documentation had been destroyed by the Home Office. The cases were legally straightforward once the policy failure was admitted. The straightforwardness was the outrage: these were not edge cases or gray areas; they were people who had every legal right to be here and were being told they didn't, by a system that had destroyed its own proof. I gave two media interviews that Tariq managed to stay professional about despite clearly wanting to use. The cases were resolved. The policy that produced them was not.

## E006 · 2020-03 → 2020-11 · intensity: 7
**COVID, the Visa Cliff**

The pandemic produced a specific legal crisis for clients on time-limited visas: employment had stopped, the fee extension process required in-person biometrics, and the Home Office was not issuing exemptions clearly or consistently. I spent six months navigating a rules framework that was being updated faster than anyone could publish guidance. Saira came on board as a junior associate in June, which I'd been planning to delay until the firm was more stable. The workload made delay impossible. She was better than I expected immediately, which should have told me something about my expectations.

## E009 · 2023-01 → 2023-10 · intensity: 7
**Saira's Challenge, the Daily Argument**

Saira and I have been in productive argument about the limits of formalism for two years. The argument is always the same in structure: she brings a case where the formal procedure produces an outcome that is technically correct and morally inadequate; I argue the procedure protected the client from a worse outcome they didn't see coming. We have been both right enough times that neither of us has convinced the other. The argument has made me more able to articulate why I believe what I believe and less certain that the articulation is complete. I have kept all the cases where Saira was right. There are nine of them.

**The Larger Firm Offer**

Miriam Gold's firm offered me a partnership in January. The financial terms were the best I'd been offered. The cultural fit was correct. The case type was compatible. I spent four months deciding.
