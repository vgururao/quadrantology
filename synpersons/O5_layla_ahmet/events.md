## E002 · 2015-04 → 2016-02 · intensity: 7
**The PCOS Diagnosis**

I was diagnosed with PCOS at twenty-three after two years of irregular cycles and a period of fatigue so persistent I'd stopped planning around it and started working through it instead, which is not a solution. The diagnosis gave me a name and a medication and, unexpectedly, a framework: my energy was not random, it was cyclical and predictable if I paid attention to it. I started tracking it precisely — a spreadsheet that had nothing to do with work and everything to do with it. I moved my highest-stakes deadlines away from the days I knew would be low. My output improved. No one at work noticed the change because to them it looked like better project planning. It was. It was also something else.

## E003 · 2017-03 → 2018-01 · intensity: 7
**PMP, the First Real PM Role**

I passed the PMP exam in March 2017 and was moved into a project management role at the firm I was at — a B2B logistics software implementation, my first full PM scope. The scope was larger than my experience, which was deliberate on my part: I'd read the job description and concluded I could do it in six months and that the six months of discomfort was the faster route to competence. I was right about the first part and underprepared for the second. The client was a freight company with operational complexity I hadn't modeled correctly. He said: most PMs don't fix it. That was the most useful professional exchange I had that year.

## E007 · 2022-07 → 2023-06 · intensity: 8
**Cem's Architecture, My Problem**

The project I was assigned in July 2022 had been running for eight months under a different PM. The architecture had been specified by Cem Yıldız — an unconventional microservices structure that the client's infrastructure couldn't support at the required throughput. The previous PM had flagged the issue. Cem had said the implementation was the problem, not the architecture. I was handed the project as an implementation problem. I spent three weeks in the system and understood that it was an architecture problem. I could not say this in any document, any meeting, any handover note. The professional fiction required was: execution improvements will address the throughput. The actual work was: quietly redesign the most structurally broken interfaces while calling it optimisation. I called it optimisation in every status report. The system stabilised by June 2023. Cem said: good PM work. I said: thank you.

## E009 · 2023-08 → 2024-01 · intensity: 6
**Kerem, the Problem Method**

Kerem Şahin was the tech lead I inherited on the restructured project. My first month I gave him detailed specifications and watched him implement adjacent to them — not wrong, but not what I'd specified, because what I'd specified wasn't interesting enough to hold his attention. I adjusted my approach in month two: I stopped giving him specifications and started giving him problems. The problems were the same requirements written as constraints rather than instructions. His outputs improved immediately. He came to me in October and said: why do you give me problems instead of plans? I said: because you solve problems. He said: most PMs give me plans. I said: I know. He said: don't stop doing this. I said I wouldn't. I've thought since then about whether I'm managing him or accommodating him and whether there's a useful difference.
