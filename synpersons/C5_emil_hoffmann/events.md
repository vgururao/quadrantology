## E001 · 1990-06 → 1991-04 · intensity: 8
**Medical Degree, The Wrong Kind of Doctor**

I finished the MD and immediately knew I wasn't going to practice. This was not a crisis; it was a clarification. The clinical rotations had shown me that I was most interested in the questions medicine couldn't answer yet — the population-level patterns, the statistical anomalies, the cases that didn't fit the models. I told my parents over dinner that I would continue to a PhD in epidemiology. My father, a retired civil engineer, looked at me for a long time and then said: you've spent six years learning to help individual people and now you want to help populations you'll never meet. I said yes. He said he supposed that was also necessary.

## E002 · 1993-04 → 1994-02 · intensity: 7
**The First Marriage and Its Pace**

Brigitte and I married quickly, which was the kind of thing I did in my thirties — I decided and then acted and didn't spend enough time examining whether the deciding and the acting were based on the same information. She was a physician. We worked in the same building for two years and were comfortable together in the specific way that competent people from similar backgrounds can be comfortable without sharing a deeper register. The children came — Sophie in 1994, Markus in 1992 — and the comfort persisted and deepened and was never quite enough. I understood this clearly about fifteen years into the marriage, which is not ideal timing for clarity.

## E003 · 1998-09 → 1999-06 · intensity: 8
**The BSE Paper, The First Real Fight**

I published a paper arguing that the British government's BSE risk communication had systematically underestimated transmission probability to protect the beef industry, and that the methodological choices weren't scientific errors — they were policy outcomes dressed in scientific language. Three colleagues refused to review future papers with me. Braun, then still a peer rather than an enemy, told me privately that I was right about the politics and should have been more careful about the implication of bad faith. I told him the implication was the argument. He looked at me with an expression I've seen on his face many times since. That exchange was the first version of a conversation we've been having for twenty-five years.

## E004 · 2005-02 → 2006-01 · intensity: 7
**Divorce, the Cottage in Bavaria**

Brigitte and I separated when Sophie was eleven and Markus was thirteen. There was no catastrophe, which made it stranger than a catastrophe would have been. We had grown into parallel lives that occupied the same domestic space and we both knew it and one day we said so. I inherited the Bavarian cottage from my uncle that same year, which gave me somewhere to go on weekends that wasn't the Munich flat she kept and wasn't a hotel. I spent a year in that cottage reading and hiking and doing the quiet work of understanding what I'd substituted for proximity. Sophie came some weekends. Markus didn't, for two years.

## E005 · 2009-11 → 2011-06 · intensity: 6
**Meeting Klara, the Second Marriage**

Klara was consulting for a pharmaceutical company that had engaged me for an epidemiological review. She asked questions in a meeting that I answered with more precision than the meeting warranted, and she told me afterward that I'd explained things clearly but had underestimated what the room needed to hear rather than what was true. She was right. We had dinner and she told me she was right about other things too. I married her in 2011, which Sophie approved of immediately and Markus took longer with and eventually came around to, not entirely.

## E006 · 2014-09 → 2015-04 · intensity: 6
**The WHO Review Panel**

I served on a WHO pandemic preparedness review panel and wrote a minority report arguing that the governance framework was optimized for the last pandemic rather than the next one. The majority report was more diplomatic. Mine was filed, circulated internally, and produced no visible effect. I understand, now, that this is approximately what minority reports do. At the time I thought the correctness of the argument was the mechanism and that correctness, published, would drive revision. I was fifty years old and still thought that. Sophie told me much later that this was the thing about me she'd most inherited from me and most wanted to return.

## E007 · 2019-07 → 2020-03 · intensity: 7
**COVID Arrives and the Thesis Is Ready**

I had been modeling pandemic response decision-making for twenty years. When the initial Chinese outbreak data arrived in January 2020, I had a framework that made predictions I wrote down privately so I could check them afterward. The predictions about government response — the overclaiming, the coordination failure, the treatment of uncertainty as certainty — were more accurate than I wanted them to be. I was already preparing a paper when the lockdowns started. The paper and the lockdowns arrived simultaneously, which seemed like vindication and turned out to be the beginning of the most professionally damaging period of my life.

## E008 · 2020-04 → 2020-11 · intensity: 8
**The Lockdown Critique and the Allies**

I published a critique of the German lockdown framework in July 2020 arguing that the decision-making process had conflated epidemiological modeling with policy prescription in ways that would be costly and would not be honestly evaluated. Braun responded publicly and specifically. Within three months I had been cited approvingly by people I did not want to be cited by: anti-vaccine influencers, political opportunists, conspiracy websites in three languages. I published a clarification. The clarification was ignored by everyone who had misused the original and read carefully only by the people already reading me carefully. This is the mechanism no one warns you about: the right argument in the wrong context, and then the context is all that anyone remembers.

## E009 · 2021-04 → 2023-01 · intensity: 8
**The COVID Episode and Working Sick**

I contracted COVID in April 2021. The acute phase was ten days. The fatigue that followed lasted eighteen months. I worked through it, which in retrospect was a series of decisions I would not make again. By the following spring I was producing analysis at reduced quality and not admitting it. Isabella — Dr. Koch — started flagging errors in drafts that I would previously have caught myself, and she did it without comment, which was more useful than any comment would have been. I recovered fully by early 2023. The recovery changed my analysis of long COVID: I had been skeptical of some case definitions; I became significantly less skeptical, not because the data changed, but because I got inside the experience. That's not how science is supposed to work. It's how it sometimes does.

## E011 · 2022-11 → 2023-07 · intensity: 7
**Braun's Open Letter**

Braun organized a letter signed by thirty-four colleagues calling for a review of my LMU visiting appointment on the grounds that my public communication during the pandemic had been irresponsible and potentially harmful. The letter was published in a medical journal. I wrote a response that ran in the same journal. My response was careful. I am aware, looking back, that it was the most careful writing I produced during that period — I was not going to give them an additional error to cite. The response was read widely. The appointment was renewed. The thirty-four signatures have not shifted. Klara said I should take some time off. I did not.

## E012 · 2023-08 → 2024-04 · intensity: 5
**Sophie's Paper, the Collaboration**

Sophie published a social science paper examining how scientific institutions managed uncertainty communication during the pandemic. It cited my pandemic critique approvingly on two points and challenged a third point directly. The challenge was in her data and was a good challenge. We co-authored a response piece that addressed all three points, which required me to say in public that my daughter had found a flaw in my argument. I said so. The co-authored piece is the work I'm proudest of in the last three years, partly because of the finding and partly because of what it meant to write it with her.

## E014 · 2024-10 → 2025-06 · intensity: 6
**The New Paper and the New Allies Problem**

I published a paper on excess mortality measurement methodology in late 2024, arguing that the standard approach systematically undercounts in low-income populations. It was careful, peer-reviewed, and cited accurately in two serious epidemiological outlets and then picked up by a far-right website arguing that COVID vaccines were killing people. The mechanism, again. I published a one-paragraph clarification. Klara said: you can't solve this by writing more clearly. I said I can't stop trying. She said she knew. That exchange has happened in various forms for fifteen years and neither of us has changed positions.

**The LMU Renewal, the Longer View**

The visiting appointment at LMU was renewed for three more years in September. The department chair told me, with genuine warmth, that the work remained important and the noise was not within the faculty's control. I thanked her and walked back to my car thinking about Braun's letter and what thirty-four signatures means about the world I'd chosen to occupy. Not regret. More like: I knew this was the cost and I chose it, and I'm still choosing it, and I'm clear about that now in a way I wasn't at forty. The clarity doesn't make it easy. It makes it mine.
