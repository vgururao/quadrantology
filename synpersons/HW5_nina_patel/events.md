## E001 · 2014-05 → 2015-07 · intensity: 8
**IIT to Imperial, the Distance**

I left Surat for Bombay at seventeen and Bombay for London at twenty-one. Each departure was harder than I'd expected and better for me than the place I'd come from could accommodate. Imperial College was the first time I'd been in an institution that took the technical problems of environmental systems seriously as science rather than as development economics. I had been told my entire education that climate was a problem of national will and international negotiation; at Imperial I learned it was also a problem of engineering decisions, which meant I could be part of the solution in ways that didn't require me to first become a politician. This was the most useful recalibration of my working life.

## E002 · 2015-09 → 2016-06 · intensity: 7
**The MSc Dissertation, The NGO Summer**

My dissertation modeled particulate matter dispersal in the Ganges industrial corridor. My supervisor said it was the most technically rigorous work submitted by an Indian student in five years and asked if I'd considered an academic career. I considered it for two weeks and decided I needed to work on problems I could touch rather than problems I could model. I joined a Mumbai environmental NGO in June 2016 and spent fourteen months doing groundwater monitoring in Marathwada. I made less money than I'd spent on my university housing in London. It was the most important year of my twenties.

## E003 · 2017-09 → 2018-07 · intensity: 7
**The Reliance Decision**

I took the Reliance job in September 2017. The NGO salary was unsustainable; the student loan was outstanding; my father had not said anything direct but his silence on the question of my finances was more eloquent than his words. Kiran — who became my flatmate that year — said: it's not a betrayal, it's a strategy. I said I wasn't sure those were mutually exclusive. I started the role knowing I was trading an honest position for a leveraged one, and that the leverage would only matter if I used it. I made myself a private promise about what using it meant. I have kept it imperfectly, which is how promises are kept.

## E004 · 2019-04 → 2020-02 · intensity: 6
**The Diwali Season and the Asthma**

In November 2019 the Mumbai particulate levels reached 300 μg/m³ for four days after Diwali. I was hospitalized briefly for an asthma episode. I was discharged with a new salbutamol prescription and an internist's note recommending I reduce outdoor exposure during peak pollution periods, which I read as "live somewhere else" in technical language. The health event coincided with a sustainability reporting cycle at Reliance where I was generating the data that would eventually appear in a document I was also responsible for reviewing. I sat in the hospital and thought about the specific absurdity of this and then went back to work.

## E005 · 2020-03 → 2020-10 · intensity: 7
**COVID, the Air, the Data**

Mumbai's lockdown from March 2020 produced the cleanest air quality data the city had recorded since systematic monitoring began. I documented it obsessively. The correlation between industrial activity reduction and particulate improvement was the most direct evidence I'd collected in three years of corporate monitoring. I submitted it as an internal research memo arguing that sustained emissions reductions were technically achievable. Vikram Shah said the memo would be useful for the long-term planning process. I asked when the long-term planning process was. He said it was ongoing. I filed the memo and kept a copy.

## E006 · 2020-11 → 2021-08 · intensity: 6
**Kiran's Dinner Arguments**

Kiran and I had been flatmates for three years and our dinner conversations had settled into a productive friction that I looked forward to more than any professional meeting. She covered ESG as a skeptic; I practiced ESG from the inside as a believer who had to prove her beliefs every quarter. She challenged me on the causal links I assumed; I challenged her on the discount rate she applied to long-term physical risk. Neither of us has convinced the other. Both of us have gotten better at our own arguments for having had them regularly tested. I would not have stayed in the corporate role as long if Kiran's dinner questions hadn't kept the work honest.

## E007 · 2021-09 → 2022-05 · intensity: 7
**The Internal Report, the Block**

I submitted an internal report in September 2021 recommending a mandatory scope 3 emissions disclosure framework for Reliance's chemical division supplier network. Vikram reviewed it, proposed two substantial amendments that softened the recommendation from mandatory to voluntary, and asked me to revise. I submitted the original version with a covering note explaining why the amendments compromised the finding. The report was tabled. Arjun Kapoor called me into his office and said: you know I protect you where I can. I said I know. He said: that's the limit of where I can. I said: I know that too. He said: okay, then we understand each other.

## E008 · 2022-02 → 2022-09 · intensity: 6
**My Mother's Protest**

My mother led a three-month campaign against a textile factory's water discharge into the local aquifer in Surat. She didn't describe it as activism; she described it as doing what the situation required. She was interviewed in a local newspaper and the interview reached me via a WhatsApp forward. I read it and called her. She described the campaign in detail — the water tests she'd organized, the meetings, the petition to the municipal authority. I told her she was doing better environmental monitoring than most of the people I worked with. She said: I'm just doing what anyone would do if they were paying attention. I told Kiran that evening and Kiran said: you see where it comes from.

## E009 · 2023-01 → 2023-08 · intensity: 6
**The Imperial Reunion and What It Showed**

I attended an Imperial College sustainability programme reunion in London. Twelve people from my cohort who had gone into different sectors. Three had remained in NGOs. Four were in government roles. Five, including me, were in corporate sustainability. The corporate five had the most technical leverage and the most institutional constraint. The NGO three had the least leverage and the most credibility. We spent a dinner discussing this without resolving it. The conversation was the most honest one I'd had in years about whether my strategy was working. The answer was: partially, which is both more and less than I'd hoped.

## E010 · 2023-09 → 2024-04 · intensity: 7
**The Scope 3 Report, Public**

The Securities and Exchange Board of India published new mandatory sustainability disclosure requirements in September 2023. The requirements were close to what I'd proposed internally in 2021. I submitted a revised version of my internal report through the official SEBI consultation process as public comment. It was cited in three responses from industry bodies. Vikram Shah read the public comment and said nothing to me directly. Arjun called me and said: you found a door I didn't know was open. I said: the door was always open. He said he supposed that was right. The SEBI consultation meant my recommendation was now part of the regulatory record rather than a tabled internal document.

**The Bhagavad Gita and the Practical Question**

I've been reading the Gita since I was a child. The Gandhian interpretation — karma yoga, right action without attachment to outcome — is the framework I operate from professionally. This year I read it in a context where the attachment question was live: whether to stay in a role where the leverage was real but slow, or to move to a position where the impact was visible but limited to what the institution could already see. My mother said: pray more, be less clever about it. Kiran said: you're asking the same question I've been asking about my fund for three years. Reliance announced a public emissions reduction target in August 2025 that included a version of the scope 3 disclosure framework I'd been working on since 2021.
