## E001 · 2018-05 → 2018-09 · intensity: 8
**KNUST Graduation and the Immediate No**

I graduated KNUST in May and applied to four companies, two in Accra and two abroad. All four declined. The Accra ones cited insufficient experience. One international company's auto-rejection arrived eleven minutes after I submitted. I spent the summer building things that hadn't been asked for: a load-testing tool for low-bandwidth environments, a parser that handled the specific character encoding problems that were breaking half the financial apps deployed in West Africa. I put them on GitHub. Three months later, a Berlin developer emailed to say the parser had saved his team a week of work. I didn't have a job, but something I'd made had traveled to a place I'd never been.

## E002 · 2018-10 → 2019-06 · intensity: 7
**The Stackfield Offer, the Hesitation**

Felix Wagner emailed me about the parser in October. By December I had a job offer. The salary in euros was more money than my father earned in a year as a welder in Kumasi. I called my mother. She said praise God and also asked what the company made. I said software tools for businesses. She said: and this helps people? I said: it helps the businesses. The call was quiet for a moment. I took the job. The hesitation wasn't about the money. It was about the question she'd asked and the answer I'd given.

## E003 · 2020-03 → 2020-09 · intensity: 7
**COVID, Remote Work in Accra**

When Stackfield went fully remote in March 2020, the practical advantage shifted. I was already remote and my connection was already the limiting factor in every team call. I spent six months building a local caching layer that let me work with acceptable latency on a 5 Mbps connection — tools I couldn't contribute back to the company because they were workarounds for infrastructure problems the company didn't acknowledge having. Kofi moved home during that period when his engineering program went online, and we lived together in the family house in Kumasi for three months, which was the longest we'd been together since I was eighteen. I missed Accra. I was glad to be there.

## E004 · 2021-01 → 2021-07 · intensity: 8
**The Open-Source Library, The Traction**

I released a library for handling distributed state synchronization in low-bandwidth peer-to-peer networks. It addressed a specific set of problems that were mostly invisible to developers in Europe or North America but fundamental to anyone building for Sub-Saharan African infrastructure conditions. Within six months it had 840 GitHub stars, integration in four independent projects, and a citation in a paper from a university in Nairobi. I told Ama about it over beer in Osu. She said: now make sure you document the ownership. I said it was open source, attribution was in the license. She said: I didn't say attribution, I said ownership. That conversation sat with me.

## E005 · 2021-10 → 2022-04 · intensity: 8
**Bjorn's Fork, the Realization**

I found Bjorn's fork in October. He'd taken the library, removed the attribution, added a commercial licensing wrapper, and was selling it to Nordic enterprise clients. The license was MIT, which technically allowed commercial use — the attribution removal was where the breach was clear. I emailed him. He replied that he'd done nothing wrong and that my concerns about commercial use were "ideological rather than legal." I called Ama. She said: I told you. Felix was sympathetic and then legally cautious. I retained an attorney in Berlin, which took four months of savings and a loan from Kofi I haven't fully repaid.

## E006 · 2022-06 → 2023-01 · intensity: 6
**The Community Rallies**

When I posted publicly about the fork dispute, the response was larger than I'd expected. Developers from Kenya, Nigeria, India, and Indonesia shared similar experiences. A developer in Bangalore had been forked three times in two years. The conversation became something larger than my specific case — about who owns the infrastructure of the open-source ecosystem and whether the MIT license serves developers from the Global South as well as it serves everyone else. I was surprised to find myself at the center of a policy conversation when what I'd wanted was a legal outcome. These are not the same thing.

Stackfield promoted me to senior engineer in January. The salary increase was meaningful and Felix said publicly that my work on the core product was the reason. I was also, simultaneously, in active litigation with a developer the company had declined to comment on. These two facts occupied the same week. I told Felix I appreciated the promotion. He said he hoped the other situation would resolve soon. Neither of us said what the "other situation" would resolve into. The promotion was genuine recognition. The silence around it was also a thing.

## E010 · 2024-01 → 2024-07 · intensity: 6
**Bjorn Settles**

The legal case settled in January 2024 — partial attribution, a licensing fee, no formal admission. My attorney called it a reasonable outcome given the MIT license baseline. I called Ama and told her. She said: and now? I said: and now I change the license on everything I write going forward. She said that was probably the most useful thing I could do. I updated my GitHub profile that afternoon and changed the license terms on all new repositories to a non-commercial variant with explicit terms for commercial adaptation. Six developers wrote to say they were doing the same.

## E011 · 2024-04 → 2024-09 · intensity: 4
**The Church Network Project**

My pastor asked me to build a simple database for the Presbyterian church's community meal programme — they were tracking dietary needs on paper and it was failing. I built it in a weekend. It's the most used thing I've ever made, by people who don't know and don't need to know how it works, which is the point. My mother heard about it in Kumasi through a church connection and called to say she was proud. She said it in a way that was different from when she says she's proud about Stackfield. I sat with the difference and decided both versions were fine.

I released a new library in November under the new license terms.
