## E001 · 2005-05 → 2005-10 · intensity: 8
**GIMPA Graduation, the Civil Service Offer**

I graduated from GIMPA in May with a degree in public administration and two options: a private sector management trainee position at an Accra bank that paid nearly three times more, or a junior posting to the Ministry of Roads and Highways through the national graduate scheme. My mother sat with me at the kitchen table in Kumasi and said: the bank will teach you to count other people's money. The ministry will teach you to count what Ghana actually has. I took the ministry posting. The first six months were the specific education of watching how the gap between policy documents and physical roads was filled — and by whom, and on what terms. I've been thinking about that gap ever since.

## E002 · 2006-09 → 2008-07 · intensity: 7
**LSE, the Frameworks, and the Botswana Question**

The LSE MSc was funded by a Commonwealth scholarship and I arrived in London knowing I was going back. The degree gave me frameworks for what I'd seen at the ministry: institutional economics, development finance, public investment management. The seminar that shaped my thinking most was on comparative development models. The professor asked why Botswana had built functional institutions from resource revenues when most comparable countries hadn't. The answer had everything to do with what the rules said and whether people actually followed them. I wrote my thesis on infrastructure investment governance in West Africa. My supervisor said it was workmanlike. He was right and I was annoyed. What it was, was honest.

## E003 · 2009-03 → 2010-01 · intensity: 7
**First GIIF Posting, First Real Project**

I joined the Ghana Infrastructure Investment Fund in early 2009 — it was new, underfunded, and uncertain whether it would outlast the next election cycle. My first project assignment was a feeder road program in the Brong-Ahafo region. The financing structure was straightforward; the procurement process was not, because two of the three shortlisted contractors had submitted identical cost structures in different envelopes. I flagged the discrepancy in writing. My supervisor at the time — a careful man named Ameyaw — said: you're right to flag it, and you're going to make enemies doing this, and you should keep doing it. He retired in 2014. I still use his framing.

## E004 · 2012-08 → 2013-06 · intensity: 7
**Meeting Akua, the Agreement**

Akua was covering a GIIF public briefing for the Daily Graphic in August 2012. She asked the question at the end that nobody else asked: what's the variance between projected and actual disbursement on the northern corridor program, and what's the explanation? I said I'd get her the numbers. I got her the numbers. We had coffee. The coffee lasted three hours, and by the end of it I understood that the conflict between what I did and what she did was structural and permanent — she followed the evidence in infrastructure procurement stories, and I worked in infrastructure procurement. We talked about it directly before we'd been dating four months. The agreement we made — no discussion of specific GIIF projects, no sourcing, no backgrounding — has held. The discipline required is real and we both provide it.

## E005 · 2015-03 → 2016-01 · intensity: 6
**The Northern Corridor Assessment**

I was assigned to lead the financing assessment for a major northern corridor transport project — roads, bridges, a small rail component. The World Bank co-financing required rigorous project appraisal. I ran the appraisal carefully: traffic projections, maintenance cost modeling, fiscal risk. The government's internal demand figures were thirty percent higher than mine. I presented both sets of projections to the steering committee with a memo explaining the methodology difference. The minister at the time asked which figures we were filing with the Bank. I said: the ones I can defend, which are mine. He said: file them both. I filed mine and explained the discrepancy in a footnote. The Bank approved the project on my figures. The traffic came in at mine. I reported this to no one particularly.

## E006 · 2017-04 → 2017-11 · intensity: 6
**The East Legon House**

Akua and I bought the house in April. The decision required more financial commitment than was comfortable for a government salary, and the mortgage terms in Ghana in 2017 were not kind. My mother came from Kumasi to see it. She walked through every room the way she used to walk through a fabric stall — assessing what was real and what was presentation. She said: good bones. She said it in Twi and she meant it literally about the construction and also as general approval. My father-in-law Yaw came separately and asked about the conveyancing documents with the attention of a man who had spent thirty years watching what happened when titles weren't clean. They were clean. I had made certain of that.

## E007 · 2018-06 → 2019-04 · intensity: 7
**Sarpong's Arrival, the Optimism Problem**

Minister Sarpong was appointed in mid-2018. He arrived with a transformative vision: Ghana's infrastructure gap could be closed in one political cycle if the financing was structured boldly. The vision was compelling and the timeline was not realistic. I prepared the implementation feasibility analysis with full honesty about the capacity constraints — procurement timelines, environmental assessments, contractor pool depth. He received the analysis and said: I understand your caution and I need you to find the path that makes this possible. That's a different instruction than ignore the constraints. I spent four months finding the path that made the most of it possible. The path was narrower than his announcement. The work within it was real.

## E008 · 2020-03 → 2020-10 · intensity: 6
**COVID, the Infrastructure Pipeline**

The pandemic disrupted the project pipeline in ways the financing frameworks hadn't modeled: supply chain disruptions, contractor liquidity crises, contractor-country border closures affecting equipment delivery. I worked through six months of contract amendments and force majeure negotiations. The work was technically unglamorous and operationally essential — if the pipeline collapsed, the commitments to multilateral partners collapsed with it, and rebuilding the pipeline would take years. Daniel Ofori said, during one of our lunch conversations: you're spending your career making sure that other people's ambition doesn't destroy itself faster than it can produce results. I said: that's a harsh way to describe project management. He said: I know, that's why I said it to you and not in a memo.

**Adwoa's Visit, the Market Logic**

My mother came to Accra for a month in August — she does this every few years, a month in the city with us before returning to Kumasi to her routines.
