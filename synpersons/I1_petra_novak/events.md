## E001 · 2005-07 → 2006-04 · intensity: 8
**The VŠE Thesis and the Auditor's Shadow**

My MSc thesis examined revenue recognition anomalies in Czech telecom companies from 1999-2004. My supervisor said it was technically excellent and politically sensitive, which he meant as a warning. I understood it as a description of interesting work. The thesis required me to track down secondary documents that the companies had filed with the Czech Securities Commission and that nobody had compared side-by-side before. The comparison showed systematic timing differences. I found three cases where the differences were too consistent to be random. I reported what I found and did not speculate beyond the evidence. My father read a summary and asked what "too consistent to be random" meant in legal terms. I told him it meant I needed more data before I knew what it meant in any terms.

I sat the CFE examination in Prague and passed on the first attempt. The study materials were in English, which required that I build a technical vocabulary in a second language simultaneously with the conceptual vocabulary of fraud examination. I passed both tests at once, which I've found a useful metaphor for forensic work generally — you're always working at two levels simultaneously. Tomáš Horák was in my study group for the CFE. We argued about methodology throughout the preparation and agreed by the final week that our arguments were more productive than our agreements. We co-founded the firm the following year.

## E004 · 2012-06 → 2013-03 · intensity: 7
**The First Significant Case**

Novak & Horák was retained by a minority shareholder in a mid-size Czech construction company who suspected the majority shareholder was siphoning funds through a consulting subsidiary. The investigation took seven months. The document trail was meticulous in some areas and suspicious in others. The suspicion was in the gaps — the places where the documents stopped in an unusual way. I built a model for the legitimate billing patterns and then mapped the anomalous ones against it. The gap was approximately CZK 45 million. The shareholder litigation was successful. Horák managed the client relationship throughout; I managed the evidence trail. We've divided work the same way since.

## E006 · 2019-04 → 2020-01 · intensity: 7
**The Slovak Company, the Pressure**

An engagement in Bratislava: a Slovak manufacturing company engaged us through a referral to review their audit trail ahead of a merger. By month three I had found what I characterized in my preliminary findings as "evidence of systematic accounts payable manipulation." The company's CEO called Horák and asked if the findings could be presented differently. Horák relayed the request. I said the findings were what they were. The CEO terminated the engagement and retained a different firm. I spent two weeks following the merger announcement six months later to see what the different firm had found. Their clean audit passed through unchallenged. This is the outcome I documented in my professional notes and said nothing else about in public.

We took eleven engagements in twelve months. Jana Horáková handled four of them independently for the first time. She was as methodical as advertised — more patient with document volume than I am, which is rare. I told Horák in January 2021 that Jana was the most valuable person in the firm who wasn't a partner. ## E009 · 2022-04 → 2023-01 · intensity: 8
**The Dvořák Discrepancy**

Miroslav Dvořák hired us in October 2021 to support his position in a shareholder dispute. He expected technical validation. By April 2022 I had found a discrepancy in the fund allocation records that complicated his position significantly — the documents he had framed as evidence of the other party's misconduct contained entries that, when cross-referenced against third-party financial records, suggested a more complicated bilateral picture. I presented the preliminary finding to Dvořák in May. He asked if the finding could be excluded from the final report. I told him it could not. By September he had instructed his attorney to explore terminating the engagement. I was determining my professional obligations under CFE standards.

## E010 · 2023-01 → 2023-08 · intensity: 7
**The Obligation Decision**

The CFE code is clear on some points and requires judgment on others. The Dvořák situation was in the judgment area: the finding was material to the dispute, the client had hired us and had the right to terminate the engagement, but the evidence in my possession was relevant to a court proceeding that was still active. I consulted with the ACFE ethics line and with a Czech attorney. I completed the report and informed Dvořák that the report would be filed with the court as a professional matter regardless of whether he accepted it as a client document. He terminated the engagement. The report was filed. The court accepted it as neutral forensic evidence. The case is still in process.
