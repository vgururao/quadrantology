## E001 · 1990-08 → 1991-06 · intensity: 8
**The Arrival and the First Parish**

I came to the US from Novi Sad at nineteen with my father's address in Philadelphia, a suitcase, and a Fordham enrollment letter my uncle had helped secure. My English was functional and my philosophy professor said my arguments were strong and my rhetoric was excessive. He meant excessive as a criticism. I heard it as a description. Holy Trinity Serbian Orthodox Church in Philadelphia was the first community that felt like home — not because it was Serbian, though that mattered, but because it organized life around the premise that what you believed had consequences for how you acted, and that both things were serious. I have not left that premise since.

## E002 · 1993-05 → 1994-09 · intensity: 8
**Notre Dame, the Casework That Found the Career**

I graduated Notre Dame Law in May 1993 and spent fourteen months as a clerk for a federal judge. The case that found my career was a pro bono assignment my supervising attorney gave me: a Sikh student suspended from public school for wearing a kirpan, the ceremonial dagger, which the school called a weapon. I built the argument from the Religious Freedom Restoration Act, which had just passed. We won. The student's father shook my hand and said: you understood without needing to be explained to. I thought about that for a long time. It told me what kind of lawyer I wanted to be.

## E003 · 1995-04 → 1996-01 · intensity: 7
**Sonja and the Firm**

Sonja and I married in April 1995. She was teaching history at a Fishtown high school and had opinions about everything, which was the most important quality. Daniel Kowalski and I opened the firm that same year, in a rented second-floor office on Frankford Avenue with two desks and a shared printer. The first year we billed enough to cover rent and not much else. Kowalski managed the money. I managed the caseload. The arrangement has held for thirty years.

## E004 · 2001-09-11 → 2001-12 · intensity: 8
**The Week After, the Work After**

I was in a court filing when the planes hit. The week after, three clients of ours — a mosque, a Muslim community center, and a Sikh family — called with incidents: surveillance, harassment, one minor assault. I took all three cases. My father called from Philadelphia and said he had heard about the attacks and asked if I was alright. I said I was. He said he had seen similar things happen to different minorities in different places and that it always started with fear and always required someone to do the legal work. I said I understood. He said: I know you understand. I'm asking if you're alright. I said yes. I wasn't sure it was true.

## E006 · 2011-03 → 2012-01 · intensity: 7
**The Catholic Hospital Case**

I took a case arguing that a Catholic hospital's refusal to perform a specific procedure violated a state civil rights statute. This put me in the position of litigating against an institution whose religious tradition I formally respect, on behalf of a patient whose rights I was equally formally obligated to protect. The case received attention in both the religious liberty and reproductive rights communities, and I was misrepresented by both. I wrote a careful op-ed explaining the distinction between defending a principle and endorsing an outcome. It was read by people who already agreed with it.

## E007 · 2016-10 → 2017-06 · intensity: 6
**Natalija's Research, the Distance**

Natalija published her first academic paper in October 2016 — a public health research critique of the RFRA's effect on vaccination rates in religious communities. It used case law I had argued in two cases. She cited both, accurately, and reached conclusions I disagreed with. I read it twice. I called her and said the methodology was sound and the conclusions were consistent with the evidence and I wished she had reached different ones. She said: I know. She said it with affection. We have been in a sustained philosophical disagreement ever since that has had no effect on the warmth of the relationship, which I consider one of my greatest achievements.

## E008 · 2019-04 → 2020-01 · intensity: 7
**The University Speech Case**

I took a case defending a professor at a Philadelphia university who had been suspended for classroom speech that the administration characterized as hostile. The professor's views were ones I found personally distasteful. The principle was not about his views. The media coverage was almost entirely about his views. I gave three public interviews explaining the distinction. Elena Mirković wrote a law review response arguing that my framing concealed the asymmetric use of religious liberty doctrine in speech cases. I responded in the same journal. We argued about it over dinner four months later when she visited Philadelphia and agreed, by dessert, that we were both right about different things.

## E009 · 2020-03 → 2021-01 · intensity: 7
**COVID, Religious Liberty, and the Worship Question**

Churches in Pennsylvania were restricted during the pandemic restrictions. I took cases for three congregations — two Christian, one Sikh — arguing that the restrictions were not religiously neutral in their application. The courts found against us in two of three cases. Father Nikolaj and I had a long conversation about whether the legal arguments had been correct and whether being correct was the right frame. He said: you argued the principle because the principle was true. I said: and we lost. He said: those are not connected the way you think they are. I am still working on that.

## E010 · 2021-06 → 2022-04 · intensity: 5
**Kowalski's Hip, the Firm**

Kowalski had a hip replacement in June 2021 and was at reduced capacity for eight months. I managed the billing and client communication for the first time in twenty-seven years of partnership. ## E011 · 2022-09 → 2023-06 · intensity: 6
**The Pro Bono Immigrant Case**

I took a pro bono case for a Ukrainian family whose asylum claim had been denied on procedural grounds I found technically indefensible. The case overlapped with the Russian invasion and I want to be honest about my motivations: the legal argument was correct independent of the geopolitics, and the geopolitics made me prioritize the case above other pro bono matters I had queued. Kowalski said nothing for a week and then said: the billing this month is light. I said: I know. He said: the case is good. I said: yes. That was the conversation.

## E014 · 2024-09 → 2025-04 · intensity: 6
**The Largest Case**

A national case about the rights of religious organizations to hire and fire on the basis of religious conduct reached a circuit I'd been working toward for fifteen years. We were not lead counsel but contributed a significant amicus brief. The brief was read. The oral argument went further in our direction than I had expected. The decision is pending. Sonja read the brief and said it was the best legal writing I'd produced. Elena wrote to say she disagreed with it entirely and was filing an amicus on the other side. I replied that I looked forward to the decision. She said she knew I did.
