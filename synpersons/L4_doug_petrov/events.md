## E002 · 2000-03 → 2001-02 · intensity: 8
**The First OMB Assignment, the Rules**

I joined OMB in 2000. The first year I spent learning that budget analysis was not primarily about numbers — it was about rules: the authorizing statutes, the appropriations language, the transfer authorities, the reprogramming thresholds. The rules were the architecture of what the government could do and what it couldn't, and the architecture had been built over 200 years and had the specific texture of accumulated human experience with what happens when the architecture fails. My supervisor gave me the Federalist Papers at my three-month review and said: read them as engineering documents, not political ones. I've read them annually since.

## E004 · 2008-09 → 2009-06 · intensity: 7
**The Financial Crisis and the Budget**

The 2008 financial crisis required OMB staff to work through a budget environment I hadn't encountered in eight years: the normal rules were being stress-tested by the speed of the emergency, and the emergency justified exceptions that would have taken months under normal conditions. I spent the fall processing supplemental appropriations requests that were legitimate and that were moving faster than the review process was designed for. I approved the legitimate ones on the reduced timeline and held the ambiguous ones for full review. My supervisor said I was being procedurally conservative in a moment that required flexibility. I said: procedural conservatism in an emergency is what the process was built for. He moved on. I kept the standard.

## E006 · 2016-11 → 2017-06 · intensity: 7
**The New Administration, the First Test**

The 2016 election result produced a transition period that tested every institutional norm I cared about professionally. By February 2017 I had seen three instances where the executive's policy team had requested actions that I assessed as inconsistent with governing statute. I documented each one and provided my statutory analysis in writing. Two were resolved in favor of my reading after escalation. One was resolved against it with a legal opinion from the incoming administration's counsel that I thought was incorrect and which I documented my disagreement with in the file. This is what career civil service is: the document in the file, against the day when the document might matter.

## E007 · 2019-04 → 2020-01 · intensity: 8
**The Popular Project Block**

A major infrastructure project came through for preliminary review in April 2019. The procurement methodology had been designated as a test pilot for a new contracting structure that had not received statutory authorization through the normal appropriations process. It was popular. It had congressional support. It had public support. The authorizing statute didn't cover it. I wrote a finding that the procurement was inconsistent with 41 USC 1303. My supervisor asked if I was certain. I said I was. He asked if I understood the political exposure. I said I understood my regulatory obligation. The block stood. The exposure was real and immediate and I received three emails I've kept in a folder labeled "evidence of why the rules exist."
