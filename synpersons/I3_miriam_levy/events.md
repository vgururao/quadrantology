## E001 · 1990-09 → 1991-06 · intensity: 8
**Hebrew University, The First Study**

My BA was in psychology but my first real study was on my parents. My father spent Fridays at the Holocaust memorial library. My mother kept the apartment exactly as it had been in Warsaw — the same positioning of the salt shaker, a specific photo of her sister who hadn't survived. I began studying what I called "the architecture of inherited grief" in a notebook I kept in my bedroom before I had the vocabulary to call it anything. My thesis was on social transmission of trauma. My supervisor said it was brave. I said it was just specific. The distinction between brave and specific in academic work is something I've been working out ever since.

## E002 · 1993-10 → 1996-04 · intensity: 8
**The PhD: The Evidence and the Blank**

I wrote a dissertation examining how groups respond to contradictory information about in-group behavior. Three hundred and forty participants, two experimental conditions, seven dependent variables. The methodology was clean. The findings were replicable. I defended in April 1996 and left the room with the specific knowledge that I had found a real thing and that the real thing was smaller than I'd wanted it to be. My advisor said the science was excellent and the ambition was useful as a career attribute and dangerous as a measurement standard. I've been managing that tension ever since.

## E003 · 2000-09 → 2001-05 · intensity: 8
**The Second Intifada, Tel Aviv**

The Second Intifada began in September 2000. Two of my students were in reserve service by November. A colleague's daughter was injured in the Dolphinarium bombing in June 2001. I was writing a paper on intergroup threat perception and the real world and the paper were the same subject for two years in a way I hadn't experienced before and found intellectually useful and personally impossible. I gave a lecture in December 2000 on the psychology of escalation and watched thirty faces that were living the lecture while I was giving it. I have not fully resolved what it means to research something you're inside. I've learned to say that openly.

## E004 · 2008-05 → 2009-03 · intensity: 7
**The Separation and After**

Daniel and I separated in May 2008. It was the right decision and slow to make. The marriage had been a compatible partnership that had been useful for Naomi's early years and had drifted past that function into something quieter than either of us had wanted to name. We told Naomi together when she was eight. She asked: are you sad? I said: yes and also this is the right thing. She said she understood. I think she understood more than I intended. The separation was amicable. The flat was mine; Daniel moved to Jerusalem. We've been collegially respectful for fifteen years, which sounds minimal and is actually more than most separations produce.

## E005 · 2011-03 → 2012-01 · intensity: 6
**The Replication Crisis Arrives**

I read the Bem precognition paper in 2011 and wrote three pages of methodological critique before I remembered it had already been published. The crisis that followed — the systematic review of social psychology's published literature for replication failures — was professionally difficult for my field and personally clarifying for my specific position in it. I had been careful. I had not been careful in every paper. I went back through my publications and found two that I would not have passed the current replication standards. I published a methodological note acknowledging this. It was the least-read thing I had published in a decade and one of the most important.

## E006 · 2014-09 → 2015-07 · intensity: 7
**The Ben-David Situation Begins**

Yoav Ben-David was prolific, confident, and publishing hypotheses that were interesting and not always supported by the evidence he claimed to have for them. I reviewed his first paper through JPSP in September 2014 and recommended major revisions. He revised and resubmitted. I recommended rejection. He published elsewhere and the paper was cited forty times. I reviewed his third paper two years later and recommended rejection again. By 2015 it was a field-level pattern. The editorial board asked me to recuse from his future submissions, which I found professionally insulting and technically reasonable. I recused.

## E007 · 2017-08 → 2018-06 · intensity: 7
**Naomi to MSF**

Naomi called in August 2017 to tell me she'd been accepted to MSF — her second application. She was twenty-six. I said: where? She said: South Sudan, initially. I said: for how long? She said: a year, renewable. I said: are you sure? She said: I've been sure since I was twelve and you let me visit the clinic in Haifa with you. I hadn't known that the clinic visit had been the moment. I thought about the eight-year-old who had asked if I was sad. She had been building toward this for nineteen years and I had been watching without knowing what I was watching.

## E008 · 2019-04 → 2020-01 · intensity: 6
**The Rheumatoid Arthritis**

I noticed the stiffness in my hands in April 2019. By November I had a diagnosis, a methotrexate prescription, and a physiotherapy plan. The adjustment was more practical than psychological — I already knew how to adapt research methods; this was just an addition to the category. The handwriting changed. The typing changed. The swimming started. What I didn't expect was that the diagnosis would produce a shift in how I thought about the relationship between physical limitation and cognitive function — I started a small research program on that topic six months after the diagnosis, which is either the most investigator-brained response possible or just a coping mechanism dressed in scientific language. Possibly both.

## E009 · 2020-03 → 2021-01 · intensity: 7
**COVID, Online Teaching, the Naomi Calls**

The pandemic moved my classes to Zoom. The research stalled briefly and then accelerated when I realized the conditions provided a natural experiment I could study with appropriate IRB protocols. The calls with Naomi were the most important hours of the pandemic year — she was in South Sudan, where COVID was competing with existing infectious disease burdens and the MSF resource allocation decisions were the kind she'd been trained for and not fully prepared for. She didn't ask for advice. She asked me to listen. I listened. The distinction between those two things is the most important thing I've learned about being someone's parent.

## E010 · 2021-07 → 2022-04 · intensity: 6
**The Journal Paper, the Response**

I published a paper in JPSP in July 2021 examining the replication rate in social threat perception research across fifteen years of the literature. The finding was that approximately 60% of published effect sizes in the domain were overstated by more than 30%. The paper named no specific authors and cited the studies numerically. Avi Goldberg called me after publication and said the paper was causing "unnecessary friction with important colleagues." I told him the finding was accurate. He said he wasn't questioning the accuracy. I said: I know, and accuracy is the only relevant criterion for what goes in the journal. He said I was aware of the consequences. I said I was.

## E011 · 2022-11 → 2023-07 · intensity: 8
**October 7th, The Following**

October 7, 2023 happened while I was at a conference in Amsterdam. The first notification came at breakfast. I flew home that afternoon on the first available flight. The following weeks I cannot write about without departing from analytical language into something else. My students were called up. Naomi was in South Sudan, which meant she was safe, which produced a specific guilt about the relief. Two colleagues lost family members in the kibbutzim. I went to work every day and taught classes because I didn't know what else to do, which is what I did in 2001 also. The research on intergroup threat perception I've been doing since 1990 has never been more immediately relevant and I've never been less certain what the relevance means for action.

## E012 · 2024-01 → 2024-09 · intensity: 6
**Naomi Home**

Naomi came home in January for a month — her MSF rotation had ended. She was twenty-nine and tired in a specific way that I recognized from colleagues who had done long field postings. We spent three days doing nothing in particular, then she started asking questions about what I was reading. We had six of the best conversations I'd had in years. She returned to South Sudan in February on a new contract. I stood in the airport and thought about the eight-year-old, the twelve-year-old, the twenty-six-year-old, the twenty-nine-year-old, and the thread that connected them, and how much of that thread I'd seen and how much I'd missed.

## E013 · 2024-03 → 2024-09 · intensity: 5
**Rachel's Paper and the Lunch**

Rachel Cohen and I have been having lunch every other week for twenty years. The lunches are the most methodologically honest professional conversations in my life, because Rachel is as careful as I am and reaches different conclusions from similar data frequently enough to keep me from confusing my own rigor with certainty. In March she sent me a draft of a paper that challenged a secondary finding in my 2021 meta-analysis. She was right about the challenge. I told her over lunch. She said: I know you'd say that. I said: you could have just emailed it. She said: I wanted to see your face.

## E014 · 2025-01 → 2025-08 · intensity: 6
**The New Program**

I began a longitudinal study on the psychological mechanisms of collective grief following the October 7 attacks. The IRB application took four months. The ethics review was the most extensive I've undergone. I wanted to be careful in a domain where my own proximity to the subject was a methodological variable I needed to account for explicitly. I wrote a positionality statement for the first time — something I'd considered insufficiently rigorous in other people's research and found necessary in mine. Rachel reviewed it. She said: it's the most honest thing you've ever put in a methodology section. I said that wasn't a compliment. She said: it was both.

## E015 · 2025-09 → 2026-04 · intensity: 4
**The RA Management and the Swimming**

The methotrexate has been stable for six years. The swimming — twice a week at the Ramat Aviv pool — is the most consistent health practice I've maintained since the diagnosis. I go at 7 a.m. and the water is cold for the first thirty seconds and then not, which is the general shape of most hard things. The hands are better in warm weather and worse in winter; I've adjusted the January research schedule accordingly. Rachel asked if I was writing about the swimming for the RA paper. I said no. She said: that's a methodological omission. I told her that not every research program needed a personal testimonial section. She said: it depends what the question is.
