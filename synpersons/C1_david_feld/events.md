## E001 · 2000-04 → 2001-02 · intensity: 8
**Nasdaq Tells the Truth**

I graduated Yale thinking I understood markets. Then the Nasdaq halved and I watched every senior analyst at Goldman insist the fundamentals were intact right up until they weren't. I was twenty-two and too junior to say anything out loud, but I kept a notebook where I wrote down every wrong call they made with such confidence. That notebook was the most important thing I produced at Goldman. It taught me that the loudest voice in the room and the correct voice are different variables.

## E002 · 2001-09-11 → 2001-10 · intensity: 9
**The Towers, the Silence**

I had a 9:30 meeting scheduled in One World Trade with a client I'd been cultivating for three months. I was on the subway when it happened. The next hour is a series of images I've never been able to put in order. I spent two days trying to reach people and couldn't reach some of them, and then eventually I could, and it wasn't the ones I was most worried about who didn't answer. I went back to work the following week because I didn't know what else to do. The market didn't reopen for four days and when it did I sat at my desk and thought: this is a system with no conscience and I have chosen to operate inside it. I hadn't fully chosen until that moment.

## E003 · 2008-09 → 2009-08 · intensity: 8
**Feld Capital Opens**

I left Goldman to start the fund in the week Lehman collapsed, which everyone told me was the worst possible timing. I thought it was exactly right — the thesis was there and the window was open and if I waited for stability I'd be waiting forever. Rachel thought I was insane. She was probably right about the timing but wrong about the underlying logic. The first eighteen months were terrifying in a clean way I haven't experienced since: no partners to disappoint, no AUM to protect, just the trade and whether it was right. We were right more often than not. I didn't fully understand then what it would cost to build something around being right.

## E004 · 2014-07-03 → 2014-09 · intensity: 8
**Emma Arrives**

Emma came three weeks early and I was in London for a conference I'd insisted was non-negotiable. Rachel's mother was with her, and Rachel was fine, and Emma was fine, and I caught a flight home that night and held her the next morning. But the fact of those fourteen hours — of being the kind of person who was in London — never fully went away. Emma was immediate and complete, the least complicated thing that had ever happened to me, and I'd managed to miss the first hours of it for a meeting I can't now remember. I coached her chess club starting when she was eight. I don't know if it cancels anything out and I've stopped trying to account for it that way.

## E005 · 2017-04 → 2018-09 · intensity: 6
**The Meridian Thesis Takes Shape**

The original short idea was simple: they were overstating recurring revenue by reclassifying one-time contracts. Park found the first discrepancy; I found the second; by spring 2018 we had a full model that said the stock was worth 40% of what the market believed. I told Rachel I'd have it wrapped up in six months. What I didn't tell her — what I hadn't told myself — was that I was more interested in being right about it than in being finished with it. The thesis was the most intellectually satisfying thing I'd built since the fund's first year. I didn't notice that this was already the overlap: the fund obsession and the marriage trouble didn't start separately.

## E006 · 2016-11 → 2017-03 · intensity: 5
**The Election, the Trade**

I had a short position on two companies I thought would suffer under a regulatory rollback, and I covered them both the morning after election night because the thesis had changed. I was right about the politics and wrong about the market's direction and I made a reasonable return and felt nothing. What I remember more was Rachel watching the results come in and saying "I don't know what country this is anymore." I said something dismissive about market efficiency and she went to bed early. It was the first conversation I remember where I heard myself speaking and thought: that was the wrong response. I didn't know yet how many more there would be.

## E007 · 2020-03 → 2020-06 · intensity: 7
**The Crash and the Wrong Kind of Winning**

March 2020 was the best quarter Feld Capital had ever had. Every short position I'd been sitting on for two years paid off simultaneously and the fund was up 34% in six weeks. I remember the specific nausea of watching the position marks go up while the news was running footage of refrigerator trucks outside hospitals. I donated a significant amount that quarter and it didn't make the nausea go away. Park called it a structural vindication. I called it a coincidence of timing that I'm not willing to take too much credit for. We were right for the right reasons, but the world being right with us required a lot of people dying.

## E008 · 2021-05 → 2021-08 · intensity: 9
**Marcus Reeve, the Leak**

Park found the IP address in our document access logs first. It took three more weeks to confirm it was Marcus. I had given him access to a preliminary draft of the Meridian analysis in late 2020 because we'd been friends for twenty years and I trusted him, and he had given it to Meridian's investor relations team, which is what triggered the campaign against us. I sat with this information for a week before I said anything to anyone. What I kept coming back to wasn't the legal exposure or the fund — it was that he'd convinced himself he was doing something principled. The betrayal would have been easier if it had been purely cynical. Knowing he had a story he told himself about it made it impossible to close.

## E009 · 2021-09 → 2022-11 · intensity: 8
**Meridian's Legal Attack + Rachel Leaves**

These two things happened at the same time, which I try not to read as causation. Meridian filed in October. Rachel and I separated in November. She told me, carefully, that she wasn't leaving because of the legal case — she was leaving because she'd been living with the Meridian obsession for four years and the legal case was just a new chapter in the same story. I didn't argue with the accuracy of it. The litigation was expensive and consuming and I was paying two attorneys while also trying to run the fund through the worst PR period of its existence. I remember one week when I had three depositions, two school pickups, and a quarterly letter to write. I completed all five badly.

## E010 · 2022-06 → 2023-02 · intensity: 7
**Divorce Finalized, Apartment Split**

Rachel and I divided things as professionally as two people can who no longer trust each other's emotional register. She kept the co-op; I bought a smaller place two blocks away so the kids could walk between. The net worth drop was significant — the litigation costs ate through the liquid holdings, and buying out my half of the co-op at peak prices required selling fund units I'd rather have kept. I took the kids for a weekend in late August and we went to a beach on the Jersey Shore and I watched Emma body-surf and Daniel build an incompetent but enthusiastic sandcastle and I thought: this is actually my life, the part that isn't litigation. I hadn't thought that clearly in over a year.

## E011 · 2023-03 → 2023-06 · intensity: 6
**The A1C Number**

The internist called it "a strong signal," which is how doctors who don't want to say pre-diabetic say pre-diabetic. I was 45, 20 pounds heavier than I'd been at 40, and running on four hours of sleep and bad takeout through most of the litigation period. I started running three times a week almost immediately, which surprised me, because I'm not someone who usually acts on medical advice without a prolonged period of argument. I think I was just ready for a domain where the feedback loop was clean: run, sleep, eat better, the number goes down. After three years of Meridian I needed something that actually responded to input.

## E012 · 2023-09 → 2024-06 · intensity: 5
**Emma's Chess Season**

She's better than I was at her age, which I tell her approximately never because I don't want to make it about me. I coached the school club twice a week and it was the cleanest part of my calendar for the entire year — no litigation, no fund pressure, just twelve kids trying to see three moves ahead. Emma has genuine positional instinct. She lost the borough tournament semifinal on a time pressure error that wasn't a calculation error, just a speed error, and she handled it better than I would have. I drove home thinking about all the things I'm teaching her without meaning to and hoping the chess is outweighing the rest.

## E013 · 2024-04 → 2025-01 · intensity: 4
**The Fund Finds Its Footing Again**

It wasn't a dramatic comeback. It was eighteen months of not making news, which after 2021 and 2022 felt like recovery. AUM was back to approximately where it was before the litigation costs. Park and I went back to the original Meridian analysis and built a new short thesis on a different company using the same methodology, and we were more careful about document security than we'd ever been about anything. I told Park it felt like learning to cook after burning down a kitchen. He said that was a more optimistic framing than he'd expected from me. I took that as a reasonable sign.

## E014 · 2025-04 → 2025-09 · intensity: 6
**Settlement Conversations Begin**

Meridian's attorneys reached out in April. They wanted to talk. I didn't know whether that meant they were confident or desperate, and I spent three weeks trying to figure out which before I realized the answer didn't change my response. I wanted terms that acknowledged the research was valid, not just a financial settlement that let them claim it was a nuisance suit. Rachel texted me about something related to Emma's school schedule on the day I got the first offer, and I remember reading both messages in sequence and thinking: these are the two things I'm most actively failing to resolve cleanly, and they require completely different kinds of patience.
