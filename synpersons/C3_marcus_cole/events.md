## E001 · 2004-05 → 2004-09 · intensity: 8
**Howard Graduation, No Plan**

I walked across that stage in May knowing I had a journalism degree, an apartment lease running through August, and no job. I'd turned down the DC internship because the editor had told me to "add more balance" to a piece about police use of force and I'd told him what I thought of that suggestion. My grandmother called it courageous. My father called it the difference between conviction and strategy and asked how I was going to eat. Both of them were right and neither answer helped me that summer, which I spent freelancing for a local alternative paper for $75 a piece.

## E002 · 2005-07 → 2006-04 · intensity: 7
**First Big Piece, First Correction**

I wrote a reported piece on sentencing disparities in Atlanta that ran in a regional magazine. It was my first substantial published work and it had an error — a statistical misread that inflated one of the key numbers by 23%. I caught it before anyone else did, sent a correction to the editor, and spent two weeks certain my career was over. The correction ran in the next issue and the piece got more attention after the correction than before. The lesson I told myself I'd learned was: correct fast. The lesson I actually internalized was: the error won't kill you, which is not quite the same thing.

## E003 · 2009-03 → 2011-01 · intensity: 8
**The Colombia Posting**

The paper sent me to Bogotá for two years to cover drug policy and the FARC peace process. It was the most important professional period of my life and also the most consistently frightening. I learned Spanish badly and quickly. I reported things I couldn't fully understand because I didn't trust the translators and I didn't understand the grammar. I filed stories about people who were taking genuine risks to talk to me and I always wondered if my protection was adequate. A source in Cali was detained three months after we spoke. I don't know if my story was the connection. I've never found a way to stop not knowing.

## E004 · 2013-09 → 2014-06 · intensity: 6
**The Celebrated Piece**

I wrote a long essay about policing and Black identity in America that ran in a major magazine and won a national press award. My father called it the piece he'd been waiting for me to write. I spent the award ceremony thinking about what Derek Okafor later published — that I'd overstated one of my key claims about complaint data by not disaggregating by precinct type. He was right. I knew he was right before he published it. I'd known when I filed it. I had the correct figure and I used the more dramatic one because the correct figure felt like it was letting something true slip through the numbers. I don't fully forgive myself for that.

## E005 · 2016-05 → 2017-02 · intensity: 7
**The First Marriage Ends**

Kendra and I separated when Maya was two. The marriage had been wrong for a year before we named it. I was traveling too much and writing too much and explaining myself too much in print to have any explanation left at home. Maya stayed in Atlanta with Kendra; I moved to DC. I drove to Atlanta every other weekend for three years and thought of myself as a present father, and then Maya was nine and told me she'd stopped expecting me to come to things and had started being pleasantly surprised when I did. I heard that sentence and re-evaluated the entire story I'd been telling myself about what I'd built.

## E006 · 2016-11 → 2017-04 · intensity: 6
**Election Night, The Drift Begins**

I sat in the newsroom watching the results and wrote a piece overnight about what the left had gotten wrong about the white working class. It ran the next morning and got more hostile mail than anything I'd ever published, most of it from people who'd agreed with everything I'd written two years before. I thought I was following the evidence. I still think that. The question that has gotten harder to answer since then is whether following the evidence and collecting the clicks are the same thing, and whether I can tell the difference from the inside.

## E007 · 2019-06 → 2019-11 · intensity: 7
**The Car, The Back**

A driver ran a red light on Connecticut Avenue and hit me broadside in July. The L4-L5 herniation didn't show up on the first MRI; it showed up on the second, eight weeks later, after I'd been filing through pain I thought was bruising. I had the surgery in October and the recovery was four months of the worst writing I've ever produced — not because I couldn't think, but because the pain was in my thinking. Sharp-edged and specific when the pain was bad, which is not the same as clear. My editor Tatiana cut forty percent of one piece and it was the right call and I couldn't tell her that at the time.

## E008 · 2020-05 → 2020-09 · intensity: 8
**George Floyd, The Summer**

I wrote four pieces in the first three weeks. The fourth one argued that the defund movement's demand was strategically self-defeating even if analytically correct, and it ran on June 15th. My inbox was the worst it had ever been. Former colleagues stopped following me. A writer I'd admired since college published a thread about what my piece represented. My father called me and asked if I was alright and I said yes and he said he'd read the piece twice and he understood what I was trying to say and then he was quiet for a moment and said he wasn't sure the moment called for that particular thing. That silence has been with me since.

## E009 · 2021-11 → 2022-04 · intensity: 7
**Derek's Critique, Published**

Derek Okafor published a 6,000-word documented challenge to the award-winning piece in a policy journal. It was methodologically meticulous and personally devastating and I had been expecting it for nine years. I wrote a response that ran in the same journal. In the response I conceded two of his four points and challenged the other two. Tatiana edited the response for three days. She removed three paragraphs that were technically accurate and personally brutal and she was right to do it, which made me more furious than if she'd been wrong. I stand by the remaining two challenges and I think about the conceded two points more than I think about anything else I've published.

## E010 · 2022-08 → 2023-04 · intensity: 7
**Janelle, The Second Marriage**

We met at a conference in DC where I was on a panel she had helped organize. She challenged one of my answers from the moderator's seat, which is technically not what moderators do. I told her afterward it was the most interesting question I'd gotten in years. We were married fourteen months later. She told me on our wedding night that she still wasn't sure I'd answered it correctly. I told her that was part of why I'd asked her to marry me. We both laughed and we were both partly serious. The marriage is new enough that I'm still discovering where its weight-bearing walls are.

## E011 · 2023-07 → 2023-12 · intensity: 6
**Maya Sends a Correction**

She was thirteen. I'd published a piece about crime data in Atlanta and she emailed me — emailed, not texted — a link to a study that challenged one of my footnotes. Her email was four sentences: one identifying the issue, one linking the source, one noting the sample size limitation in my original source, and one asking if I'd seen it. I had not. I sent a correction note to my editor. I wrote Maya back and told her the instinct was right. She sent a thumbs-up emoji. I have it saved.

## E012 · 2024-03 → 2024-07 · intensity: 5
**The Atlantic Profile**

A reporter at a competing outlet profiled me as part of a piece about "heterodox Black intellectuals." I hated the framing the moment I read it. I spent the interview trying to escape the category and the published piece put me firmly back in it. My father read it and said nothing for two weeks, then called and said he was proud of my career. The framing of the call was slightly different from the framing of pride, and I noticed the difference, and I didn't ask him to say more. Sometimes you take what's offered.

## E013 · 2024-09 → 2025-04 · intensity: 4
**Janelle's Case, the Household Debate**

She was working a civil rights case that overlapped with a piece I was writing about structural reform. We had an unwritten rule about not discussing active work at home and we broke it twice that fall, both times productively and both times leaving the table aware we'd been talking past each other. She thinks structural change is the only lever that matters and that my contrarianism aestheticizes complexity at the cost of movement building. She's not wrong. I think movements sometimes mistake unanimity for correctness and that the person asking inconvenient questions is doing necessary work. I'm not wrong either. We're going to have this conversation for the rest of our lives.

## E014 · 2025-02 → 2025-07 · intensity: 6
**The Retraction Question**

The award-winning piece was nominated for inclusion in a journalism anthology. The editors contacted me to get my approval and I spent a month trying to decide whether to pull it. I had corrected the statistic. The piece remained influential and cited and wrong in the place I'd made it wrong. Tatiana said I was overthinking it. My father said what I thought he'd say. Janelle said the question was what my readers needed, not what made me feel clean. I gave approval with an editor's note attached. The note ran. The inclusion ran. I don't know if it was the right call.

## E015 · 2025-11 → 2026-04 · intensity: 5
**Trump, Second Term, The Column**

I published a column arguing that the left's exhaustion with democracy as a concept — the willingness to entertain questions about whether the system was worth defending — was a more urgent problem than the right's open hostility to it. It ran in January. The hostile mail was different from 2020: sharper, more targeted, more from people who know me rather than people who'd just encountered the byline. Janelle read it and said she understood every argument and disagreed with the timing. I said the timing was the point. She said that was the kind of answer that was worth examining. I'm still examining it.
