## E001 · 2007-02 → 2007-08 · intensity: 8
**The KAIST Thesis and the Patent Question**

My MS thesis demonstrated a power efficiency improvement in embedded sensor arrays that the department wanted to patent through the university. I had built the core technique by reworking an open research framework published by a Japanese lab three years earlier. The patent would have required obscuring that lineage. I told my advisor I wouldn't sign. He told me the industry partnership depended on clean IP. I told him it didn't depend on clean IP if we made the IP artificially clean. He passed my thesis without the patent. I was twenty-three and I didn't understand until much later what I'd cost him in that conversation.

## E002 · 2009-04 → 2010-02 · intensity: 7
**LG, the Goldfish Culture**

I joined LG Electronics' consumer electronics division in 2009, fresh from the MS. The first six months I spent learning that working for a chaebol meant your contributions were attributed to the team, which meant to the senior engineer, which meant to the division director by the time the annual review was written. I had three innovations in my first year that appeared in product specs under other names. I didn't fight it. I learned the system and started keeping a private technical log. My father called it a necessary accommodation. I called it useful intelligence about the gap between what institutions say they value and what they actually value.

Ji-young and I married in the autumn. ## E004 · 2016-03 → 2017-01 · intensity: 6
**Han Soo-jin and the Idea**

Soo-jin came to me with the initial reverse-engineering idea in the spring. We were troubleshooting a proprietary medical device that had failed in the field — a continuous glucose monitor that had a firmware bug the manufacturer refused to acknowledge. Soo-jin said: what if we just opened it? I said: then what? She said: then we fix it. That conversation happened in the basement of the LG building in Seoul and it was the beginning of the project and neither of us knew what kind of beginning it was. I told Ji-young that evening that I was working on something interesting. I didn't tell her what kind of interesting.

## E005 · 2018-04 → 2019-02 · intensity: 7
**The Hypertension Diagnosis**

The company health checkup flagged my blood pressure at 38. 145/92, which wasn't catastrophic but wasn't fine. The cardiologist I saw independently said it was likely stress-related and also possibly heritable and definitely manageable. I went on amlodipine and a tracking protocol. It changed how I worked: I started cycling to work three days a week, I stopped eating at my desk, I began sleeping on an actual schedule. The diagnostic result was the most useful professional development intervention of the year. Ji-young said she'd been watching the blood pressure for two years without saying so. I told her she should have said something. She said I wouldn't have listened. She was right.

## E006 · 2019-08 → 2020-06 · intensity: 8
**The Device Works, the Exposure Begins**

The first successful reverse-engineered patch deployed in a patient's device in August 2019. I won't say which patient or which city. The device performed correctly where it hadn't before. I sat with the test results for three hours before I told Soo-jin. She was already moving to the next version. The COVID lockdown in 2020 paradoxically accelerated the project — remote work freed up time I'd been spending commuting and in meetings, and the pandemic made the healthcare access argument more visible in ways I hadn't expected. Ji-young asked me in May 2020 whether I'd stopped or was still doing it. I told her I was still doing it. She asked me twice over the following year to stop. I did not stop.

## E007 · 2020-11 → 2021-06 · intensity: 6
**Min-ji Finds Out**

My sister called after reading a thread on a Korean engineering forum that was discussing anonymous work she'd connected to me from context. She didn't ask if it was me — she knew — and she said: I understand why you're doing this and I'm scared for you. She's a pediatric nurse. She told me about three cases in her hospital where access to better glucose monitoring would have changed the outcome. I told her that was exactly the problem I was solving. She said: I know, and you're going to solve it right into a prosecution. We didn't speak for two months after that, and then she called on my birthday and we never mentioned it again.

## E008 · 2021-07 → 2022-04 · intensity: 7
**Lim's Contact**

The MFDS officer made informal contact in July 2021 — a message through a mutual professional connection, brief, asking me to "consider the regulatory framework's protective function." Not a formal communication. Not yet. I showed it to Soo-jin and she said: this is the warning shot. I spent two weeks deciding what it meant for the project timeline and concluded it meant we had perhaps eighteen months before it became formal. I told Ji-young. This was the first full conversation we'd had about the project's actual risk. She cried, which she almost never does. I said I would wrap it up within a year. I believed that when I said it.

LG ran a technical review in the Consumer Electronics division and my project work scored in the top five percent. The performance review commended my systematic approach to complex design challenges. I accepted the commendation and said nothing in the review meeting about what I was doing the other sixteen hours of the week. ## E010 · 2023-03 → 2023-09 · intensity: 7
**The Decision to Slow Down**

Soo-jin and I had a direct conversation about the timeline for the first time without me managing her expectations. I told her Lim's informal contact hadn't been the only signal — there had been a second, more specific one in February. She asked what I wanted to do. I said slow down, document everything, position for disclosure through the official channel rather than public release. She said she'd been thinking the same thing for four months. We agreed on a disclosure target date of Q1 2025. I told Ji-young that evening that we were winding toward an end. She didn't say anything for a long time and then said: you're sure? I said I was as sure as I've been about anything.

## E014 · 2025-04 → 2025-10 · intensity: 7
**MFDS Review, the Outcome**

The MFDS review took six months. The outcome was complicated: they issued a finding validating the safety concern we'd identified, opened a formal manufacturer investigation, and issued us a formal caution for conducting unauthorized device modification. The caution is not a prosecution. It's a line in a file. My father read about it through a news article and called in a register I've heard only twice before. He said: you embarrassed the family. I told him I fixed a device that was harming people. He said both things could be true. He's right. I don't know yet which one will be louder, looking back.
