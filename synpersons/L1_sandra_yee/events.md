## E001 · 1997-09 → 1998-07 · intensity: 8
**Arriving in Toronto, the First Rules**

I came from Hong Kong at eighteen on a student visa that required me to maintain full-time enrollment, work a maximum of twenty hours a week off-campus, and report any change of address. I read those rules twice on the flight and memorized them, not because I was required to but because they were the first set of rules in a new country and the rules were what I had. My mother had made a list of what Canada required and what Canada expected and she gave it to me in a red envelope at the airport. I still have the list. The distinction between required and expected turned out to be the most useful framework she gave me.

## E002 · 2003-05 → 2004-06 · intensity: 8
**The Osgoode LLB and the First Real Case**

My LLB thesis was on securities fraud regulation under the Ontario Securities Act. I traced three enforcement cases where the violation was clear, the documentation was complete, and the regulatory response was procedurally compromised at the investigation stage in ways that let the violator escape on technical grounds. My supervisor said it was technically rigorous and recommended for publication. I didn't publish it because I was starting articles and didn't want to be known for criticizing the regulator I might work for. This was the first time I consciously traded a correct position for a strategic one and I still think about it when I counsel other young lawyers on publication timing.

## E003 · 2008-01 → 2009-02 · intensity: 7
**The 2008 Crisis and What It Revealed**

I was in my second year as a compliance associate when Lehman fell. The crisis produced a specific educational experience: I watched large financial institutions behave in ways that were not illegal under the rules that existed, that were clearly contrary to the purpose the rules were designed to serve, and that the rule-makers had failed to anticipate. This was the most philosophically clarifying event of my early career. I had believed that rules, carefully written and properly enforced, prevented the worst outcomes. The crisis showed me that rules were necessary and not sufficient and that the enforcement culture mattered as much as the text. I started paying attention to enforcement culture specifically.

## E004 · 2010-07 → 2011-04 · intensity: 7
**Michael, the Houses**

Michael and I had been together for four years when we bought the North York house. He managed the physical construction details — the inspection, the renovation schedule, the contractor relationships — with the same systematic competence he applied to his construction projects. I managed the legal due diligence, the title review, and the mortgage documentation. We divided the house along the lines of our respective expertise and arrived at a result neither of us could have achieved alone, which is the most accurate description of our partnership I have. The mortgage is fifteen years from paid off and I've been counting since the day we signed.

## E005 · 2014-04 → 2015-01 · intensity: 7
**The Meridian CCO Role**

I was appointed Chief Compliance Officer at Meridian in spring 2014. The promotion had taken six years of documented results in compliance roles at two firms and a reputation for procedural exactness that some colleagues described as excessive and that I described as the job. ## E006 · 2017-03 → 2017-10 · intensity: 6
**My Mother's Visit, the House**

My mother flew from Hong Kong for the first time since Michael and I bought the house. She walked through every room with the focused attention she'd given everything since I was a child. In the kitchen she picked up the salt shaker and repositioned it slightly. In the garden she asked about the drainage gradient. In my home office she looked at the bookshelf for a long time. On the last evening she said: you built this correctly. She meant the house. She also meant other things. I said: I had good instructions. She said: yes, and you followed them. That was the closest thing to a direct compliment she'd offered in thirty years and it landed with full weight.

## E007 · 2019-09 → 2020-06 · intensity: 7
**The Anomaly in the Fund Statements**

Lena flagged the first anomaly in the fund statement reviews in September 2019 — a timing difference in transaction records that was small and consistent. Consistent is the word: the difference appeared in the same reporting window over six quarters. I reviewed it myself. Carver had signed off on the reporting methodology that produced the difference. I began a structured internal investigation in October and said nothing to anyone except Lena. The investigation was running when COVID locked down the offices in March 2020. We moved it to encrypted remote channels. The isolation, paradoxically, made the investigation cleaner.

## E008 · 2020-09 → 2021-07 · intensity: 8
**The Investigation Scope Expands**

By September 2020 Lena had traced the timing difference to a specific set of accounts where transaction sequencing was being manipulated to smooth quarterly performance figures. It was not a fraud in the classic sense — no money was leaving the fund — but it was a misrepresentation that affected client investment decisions. I instructed Lena to expand the document scope to the prior three years. By July 2021 we had a twelve-month document trail and a clear pattern. I briefed the board's audit committee directly, without Carver's knowledge. The committee chair said she was grateful and unsurprised by the process I'd followed.

## E009 · 2021-09 → 2022-06 · intensity: 8
**Carver's Response, the Weaponized Process**

Carver discovered the investigation in September 2021 — I don't know how, though I have hypotheses. He retained external counsel and began a formal internal process challenging the scope of my investigative authority under the CCO mandate. He used the compliance procedures exactly as written. I had written several of those procedures. I spent two months preparing a rebuttal that documented every procedural step I'd taken and every authorization I'd received. The irony of a CCO defending her compliance investigation on procedural grounds against a COO who'd never respected procedures until they were useful to him was not lost on me. I said this to Michael. He said: that's the system working as designed. You're the part that's supposed to win.
