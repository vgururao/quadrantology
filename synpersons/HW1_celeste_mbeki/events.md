## E001 · 2011-07 → 2011-12 · intensity: 8
**Wits Graduation, the Plant Smell**

I graduated with a BSc in chemical engineering and immediately joined Chemworx as a graduate trainee. My first site visit was to a processing facility outside Johannesburg and the smell hit me before I'd left the car park — a specific chemical tang I recognized from my textbooks as an indicator of venting without scrubbers. I wrote it up in my site notes and my supervisor said it was within permitted thresholds. I asked how old the permit was. He said it was the permit. I understood in that moment that there was a gap between what chemical engineers were trained to prevent and what chemical companies were structured to allow, and that I was going to have to decide which side of that gap I stood on.

## E002 · 2013-09 → 2014-06 · intensity: 7
**The Promotion and the Pride**

I was promoted to process engineer two years out of graduation. I was the youngest engineer promoted in the Johannesburg division and the only woman in the promotion cohort. My father called from Durban and said he was going to tell everyone in his church. I was genuinely proud. I also knew by this point that the permit problem I'd noted in 2011 was not an anomaly — it was a feature, and the promotion was partly because I was good at my job and partly because I was better than some of my colleagues at not making the permit problem a people problem. I sat with that for a long time before I decided it was worth noting.

## E003 · 2016-06 → 2017-03 · intensity: 7
**The MBA Decision**

I enrolled at Wits Business School with a specific calculation: I needed to understand the governance structure of the company I worked for before I could do anything useful about the things I was seeing. The MBA wasn't career advancement for its own sake. It was reconnaissance. The accounting and corporate governance courses were the most useful things I had studied since undergraduate. By the time I graduated I understood exactly which board structures enabled the environmental compromises I'd been documenting, and I was more effective at the documentation because of it. My supervisor at Chemworx called it impressive professional development. He wasn't wrong about the development.

## E004 · 2018-03 → 2019-01 · intensity: 6
**Thandi's Baby, the Night Shift**

Thandi had her first baby in March. I drove to Durban for the week, the same week I was supposed to complete a process audit for the Vaal River facility. I filed the audit late. My manager was displeased. I sat with my nephew for three days and helped my sister manage the exhaustion of the first weeks and drove back to Johannesburg thinking that I had made a correct choice and that the workplace that made it a choice worth noting was not a healthy workplace. Thandi sent me a photograph of the baby in December. I have it on my desk. I look at it now more than I did before the leave.

## E005 · 2020-01 → 2020-09 · intensity: 8
**COVID and the Vaal River Data**

I was collecting effluent monitoring data in January 2020 when the pandemic began to accelerate. Lockdown compressed the site access schedule and I had extended facility monitoring access that I used to collect a more complete baseline dataset than I'd have been able to gather under normal rotation. The data was clear by May: Chemworx was discharging into the Vaal at concentrations that exceeded permit limits on three compounds. I had dates, times, and laboratory results. I spent three months sitting with it. I told no one at work. I called Advocate Dlamini for the first time in August. He said: don't tell anyone at work.

## E006 · 2021-03 → 2021-09 · intensity: 7
**The Internal Disclosure**

Dlamini advised me to make a formal internal disclosure before going external, to establish the protected disclosure record. I submitted a formal written report to the Chemworx Environmental Compliance Committee in March 2021. Van Rensburg was on that committee. He acknowledged the report and said the data would be reviewed by an independent contractor. The independent contractor's report arrived in August and found "no material regulatory breach." I had the original data. I sent it to Dr. Dube at the NGO. She confirmed the measurements independently within six weeks. The company's contractor and the independent scientist had looked at the same river.

## E007 · 2021-10 → 2022-04 · intensity: 8
**The External Report and the Administrative Leave**

I filed with the DFFE in October 2021. Van Rensburg found out within a week. I was placed on administrative leave in November with a formal investigation pending. The investigation was framed as being into my conduct, not the effluent discharge. Dlamini said this was standard. I thought he was being calm about something that warranted more alarm. The first night of the leave I sat in the Sandton flat and didn't know what to do with my hands. I was used to shift work, site visits, the texture of being a person who goes to a place and does a thing. Suddenly I had a flat and a phone and a case file. I called my father and he said he was proud. He was crying, which he almost never does.

## E008 · 2022-05 → 2023-02 · intensity: 6
**The Church, the Community**

I started attending Sunday services every week during the administrative leave. Not because I found new faith but because the church was a place where my situation was known and not the only thing about me. The Methodist congregation in Sandton had three other women in professional disputes of various kinds. We never formed a support group — we were just people who sat in the same pews and occasionally had tea afterward and didn't require explanation. That community was the thing that kept my blood pressure from getting worse than it was. Dlamini would have been glad to know it; I never told him because he'd have found a way to make it procedurally relevant.

## E009 · 2023-01 → 2023-09 · intensity: 7
**The DFFE Finding and the Company's Response**

The DFFE issued a preliminary finding in January 2023 that the discharge levels warranted formal investigation. Chemworx's legal team issued a public statement calling the allegations "procedurally defective" and citing my administrative leave as evidence of my compromised standing. Dlamini wrote a formal response. Dr. Dube published her independent data. I was named in two newspaper articles — one sympathetic, one less so. The less sympathetic one used van Rensburg as a source without naming him. I recognized his language in three specific sentences. Dlamini told me not to respond publicly. I did not respond publicly. I wrote three drafts of a response and deleted them.

## E010 · 2023-10 → 2024-04 · intensity: 5
**Thandi's Visit, the Sandton Flat**

Thandi came to Johannesburg for a week in October, without the baby — she needed the break and I needed the company. We didn't talk about the case every day, which required active management on both our parts. She cooked three times. I cleaned up after her every time because she cooks the way a force of nature cooks. On the last evening she told me she was proud of me in a way that was different from how my father had said it — not the proud-and-frightened version, but the proud-and-certain version. She said: you're going to be all right. I told her I hoped so. She said: I'm not saying I hope so. I'm saying you will be.

## E011 · 2024-04 → 2025-01 · intensity: 7
**The Settlement Offer**

Chemworx offered a settlement in April 2024: reinstatement to a different role, a non-disclosure agreement, and financial compensation. Dlamini said the financial terms were reasonable. I said the NDA made it impossible. He said the NDA was negotiable. I said non-disclosure of a public health matter was not negotiable by me personally, whatever the legal instrument said. We went back and forth for four months. I rejected the settlement in August. Dlamini said it was the most principled and financially inadvisable decision he had observed in twenty years of practice. I said I appreciated his candour. He said he was worried about my savings, which were at that point nearly depleted.

## E012 · 2025-01 → 2025-07 · intensity: 6
**The Formal Regulatory Enforcement**

The DFFE issued a formal enforcement notice against Chemworx in January 2025. It included a reference to my original internal disclosure as the initiating documentation. Van Rensburg's public statement that week said the company was cooperating fully with the regulatory process. Dlamini sent me a one-line message: "Vindication, with several asterisks." I forwarded it to Thandi and to my father. My father read the enforcement notice online before I called and rang me in tears. I told him the asterisks were still there. He said: but the first part is also there.

## E013 · 2025-03 → 2025-09 · intensity: 5
**The Return to Work Question**

The administrative leave formally ended in March when the company withdrew the investigation following the enforcement notice. I was technically eligible to return to the same role. I did not return. I took three months to decide what I wanted, during which time I lived on the settlement money I'd declined and then accepted in a revised form — the NDA had been restructured to cover internal operational details but not the published enforcement data. I spent the three months sleeping, visiting Thandi, attending church, and having conversations with Dr. Dube about what the next version of this work should look like for both of us.

## E014 · 2025-10 → 2026-02 · intensity: 6
**The NGO Offer**

Dr. Dube's organization offered me a position as an environmental monitoring technical specialist. The salary was sixty percent of what I'd made at Chemworx. I took it immediately, before thinking through the financial implications carefully, which is the kind of decision I make when the right answer is obvious and I want to act before I talk myself out of it. Dlamini said he hoped the new role would allow me to cause appropriate trouble through appropriate channels. I told him that was the plan. My father's church took a collection for me. I accepted it in a state of gratitude and embarrassment that I'm still working through.

## E015 · 2026-02 → 2026-04 · intensity: 4
**The Vaal River, Three Years Later**

The organization sent me back to the Vaal River facility in February to document current discharge levels as part of the enforcement monitoring program. I parked in the same car park where I'd parked in 2011 on my first site visit. The smell was different — not the same tang. The scrubbers have been operating for eight months. The numbers in the current data are lower than the numbers in my 2020 data set. Not clean, but lower. I drove back to Johannesburg that evening and wrote up the findings and then sat for a while with the specific feeling of a problem that hasn't been solved but has been made smaller. I'll take smaller.
