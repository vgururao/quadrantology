## E001 · 2008-05 → 2009-03 · intensity: 8
**IIT Graduation, Mumbai Versus the World**

I graduated IIT Bombay the month Lehman collapsed. I had three offers: a software role at a Bangalore startup, a Singapore bank's analyst programme, and a deferred Stanford MBA offer that would require two years of work experience first. My father said take the bank. My mother said whatever you think is right. I took the bank because the startup seemed obvious and the bank seemed wrong in an interesting way. I was the youngest analyst on the floor and I spent eighteen months watching people make decisions with other people's money using models they didn't fully understand. It was the clearest education I've ever received about the gap between institutional confidence and actual knowledge.

## E002 · 2010-08 → 2012-06 · intensity: 8
**Stanford GSB, The Thesis That Wasn't**

I wrote a thesis arguing that enterprise infrastructure investment in Tier 2 Indian cities would outperform the consensus by a factor of three within five years. My advisor said the conclusion was interesting and the methodology was thin. I fought him on it for a semester and then went and did the work and found two assumptions that were wrong. I revised, and the revised version was less dramatic and more defensible, and I was furious about losing the dramatic version for longer than I should have been. Shreya was in my cohort. She read both versions and told me the revised one was more likely to be right and less likely to be remembered, and that I should get used to that trade-off.

## E003 · 2013-04 → 2014-01 · intensity: 7
**Joining Sandstone, The First No**

I joined Sandstone as a junior associate and spent my first six months proposing investments that Arjun Mehta declined on procedural grounds. He wasn't wrong about the process — I was skipping due diligence steps because I was certain and certainty felt like permission. The third no came with a note from Arjun that said "your conviction is not your diligence." I pinned it on my monitor for four years. I also waited until I had the process right and then pushed the same thesis through properly and it returned 4.2x. I took the note down when the investment exited.

## E004 · 2016-01 → 2016-08 · intensity: 6
**Theo's First Meeting**

Theo Nakamura came in to pitch what was technically a storage infrastructure company. His opening argument was that my firm's thesis on enterprise infrastructure was based on a vendor map that was already obsolete. He brought a diagram. I challenged him on three of the premises and he had answers for all three that were better than the questions. I backed the company despite Arjun's timeline concerns. It is the best investment decision I've made to date, and it has the peculiar quality of teaching me that the founder who defeats my argument in the room is worth more attention than the one who agrees with it.

## E005 · 2016-10 → 2017-03 · intensity: 5
**Trump, the Portfolio, and the Parents' Call**

The morning after the election, I ran a portfolio scenario for potential regulatory disruption. My father called from Chennai at 6 a.m. my time to ask if I was worried. He meant professionally. My mother called twenty minutes later and said she had been praying and that whatever happened in America would not reach us if we stayed connected to what mattered. I spent the day working. By evening two of my portfolio companies had moved significantly — one up, one down. The president of one of them called me to ask what the election meant for his Series C. I told him I'd let him know when I understood it myself.

## E006 · 2019-03 → 2019-08 · intensity: 7
**Becoming Partner, Immediately Wrong**

Arjun called me into his office in March and told me I was being made partner. I was 32, the youngest partner in the firm's history, and I made my first partner-level call two months later — a mobile payments infrastructure play in Southeast Asia — and got it materially wrong in a way that cost the fund $4 million. I called Shreya. She told me that the failure was survivable and that the important question was what I'd gotten wrong about the market read. I spent a week writing up exactly what I'd gotten wrong. Arjun read it and said it was the most useful investment postmortem the firm had produced. I was grateful and still embarrassed and both of those things remained true simultaneously.

## E007 · 2019-11 → 2021-02 · intensity: 8
**The Burnout**

It happened during a partner conflict over a term sheet that I still believe I had right. The deal fell through, the argument lasted three weeks, and I stopped sleeping in any sustained way. By February 2021 I was in a therapist's office describing what I thought was stress and she walked me through a burnout assessment and I scored in a clinical range. I went home and sat on my couch and could not make a decision about what to eat for dinner. I called my mother and she flew from Chennai. I did not work for three weeks. My father left three voicemails asking if I was physically ill. I said yes, which was technically accurate and not quite right.

## E008 · 2021-03 → 2021-09 · intensity: 6
**Therapy, the Rebuild**

My therapist was the most rigorous person I'd encountered about the gap between what I claimed to believe and what my behavior actually suggested. I had told myself a story where my contrarianism was intellectual independence; she kept asking me why intellectual independence required me to work sixteen-hour days and never let anyone be right in a meeting. I didn't have a good answer. By fall 2021 I was back at full capacity, which looks like the same thing from outside and feels very different from inside. I was slower to interrupt. I was still right at roughly the same rate.

## E009 · 2022-04 → 2022-10 · intensity: 6
**Buying the Noe Valley Condo**

I'd been renting in the Mission since 2015 and the condo purchase felt like a declaration I wasn't fully certain about. San Francisco was in an uncertain moment post-COVID — half my colleagues had left for Austin or Miami — and I was buying into a market everyone was leaving. My father thought it was financially imprudent. Shreya thought it was a classic Priya hedge: bet against the consensus while everyone else runs. She meant it as a compliment. I took it as one and tried not to think about whether she was more right than I intended.

## E010 · 2022-10 → 2023-06 · intensity: 7
**Theo's Exit, the Carry**

Theo's company was acquired in October 2022. My carry from that investment was the largest check I'd ever written to myself. I transferred a portion to my parents in Chennai within the week — my mother's NGO, my father's retirement account. My father called to say it was too much and I told him it was appropriate and he said that was my grandmother's word and we both laughed. I sat with the remainder for a week before doing anything with it and thought about whether this was what winning looked like and whether winning was what I'd thought it was when I was a junior analyst watching senior people fail slowly.

## E011 · 2023-04 → 2023-09 · intensity: 5
**The AI Infrastructure Argument**

I published an internal memo arguing that most AI infrastructure investment theses in 2023 were extrapolating data center demand curves that would not survive the first commercial correction. Arjun forwarded it to three LPs. Two disagreed vehemently. One sent a note that said I was probably right and three years early, which is the kind of feedback that makes you both proud and tired. I made two investments in the adjacent space that I believe will outperform the consensus. I'm holding the contrarian position. I've been here before.

## E012 · 2023-10 → 2024-01 · intensity: 6
**October 7th and the Portfolio Call**

Two of our portfolio companies had Israeli engineering teams. The October 7th attacks happened on a Saturday morning California time. By Monday I was on calls about operational continuity, visa status, potential relocations. I ran the operational analysis and also thought about Shreya, whose partner had family in Tel Aviv. I called her before I called the portfolio companies. She said they were safe. I filed the operational memos and felt the specific inadequacy of operational thinking in the middle of a catastrophe that wasn't asking for operational thinking.
