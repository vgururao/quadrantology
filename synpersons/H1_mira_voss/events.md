## E001 · 2013-05 → 2013-10 · intensity: 8
**The Thesis Vulnerability and the CVD**

For my BSc thesis I found a memory corruption vulnerability in a widely-deployed industrial control system. I wrote it up clean, contacted the vendor, and gave them ninety days before publication — responsible disclosure by the book. They acknowledged the bug, shipped a partial patch, and then threatened legal action if I published. I published anyway, two days after the ninety days expired. The legal threat died without action. The vendor patched properly six weeks later. I learned that the threat was a test and that flinching would have been the actual loss. My mother called when she saw the news coverage and asked if I was sure I hadn't done something wrong. I said I was sure.

## E002 · 2014-09 → 2015-06 · intensity: 7
**The MSc and the Professor Who Was Wrong**

My thesis supervisor at TU Berlin had a prior position on a cryptographic protocol I was analyzing. The honest analysis showed the protocol had a structural weakness he'd helped design. I submitted the thesis with the finding intact. He passed it but included a comment that my methodology was "tendentious." I appealed the comment and had it removed from the official record after three months of university procedure that I found deeply clarifying about how institutions manage uncomfortable findings. The finding was correct. I checked it twelve times because I needed to be certain before I fought for it.

## E003 · 2017-03 → 2017-10 · intensity: 8
**The Acquisition, the Options**

The startup I'd joined after the MSc — three engineers and a product — was acquired by a larger security company. I got a retention package with unvested options and a manager I didn't choose and a company culture that was oriented toward client satisfaction over technical integrity. I stayed because the options were real money and the work was interesting and I told myself I'd leave when the vesting finished. I am still, three years later, working out which parts of that were rationalization and which were genuine calculation. Kiri told me at the time that I was good at finding reasons to stay in situations that were good enough when good enough wasn't what I'd said I wanted.

## E005 · 2020-03 → 2020-09 · intensity: 7
**COVID, Berlin, and the Disclosure Fight**

COVID lockdowns in Berlin coincided with a vulnerability I'd found in a widely-used VPN product. The vendor was slow and the product was being deployed at massive scale for the suddenly-remote workforce. I gave them seventy-two days instead of ninety. Duc — who was by then my nominal manager through the acquisition — told me to wait. I said I'd give them the ninety days I'd given them. He said the client relationship required more time. I said the user relationship required less. I published on day ninety-one. Three thousand words at midnight. Kiri brought me tea and didn't tell me I was wrong. My mother called the next morning, again.

## E008 · 2022-02 → 2022-06 · intensity: 8
**Ukraine, the Kyiv Infrastructure Attacks**

The Russian cyberattacks on Ukrainian infrastructure began before the physical invasion and I followed them technically with a focus I hadn't felt in years. I'm not Ukrainian but the attacks were on systems I understood at a fundamental level and watching them succeed because the defenders lacked the time and resources to close known vulnerabilities was the most professionally clarifying experience I'd had since the industrial control system thesis. I wrote three technical analyses for the broader security community in March. They were the most-read things I'd ever published. I donated the consulting fees from that quarter to a Kyiv-based digital security NGO.

## E010 · 2023-01 → 2023-07 · intensity: 7
**Duc's Fork, the Legal Letter**

Duc's team had taken a significant portion of a library I'd built and open-sourced, removed the attribution, and incorporated it into a proprietary product they were selling. I sent a cease-and-desist. They sent one back claiming the fork was within license terms, which it was not. I stopped communicating with Duc directly. I retained an attorney. The technical comparison document took two weeks to build and it was the most careful work I produced that year — twelve pages of diff analysis that made the copying undeniable. The legal process is slow. My reputation in the community has not waited for it.

## E012 · 2024-04 → 2024-10 · intensity: 5
**The Industrial Control Comeback**

A client hired me to audit an industrial facility's control system security — the same category of system I'd written my BSc thesis on. Eleven years of new vulnerabilities on top of the old architecture. I filed a 94-page report and gave the client a clean disclosure timeline and they followed it almost perfectly. The vendor patched in sixty days. I wrote a short public summary. It was routine work by current standards and it was the best version of what I'd set out to do eleven years ago. I noted this in my journal and left it there.
