## E001 · 2011-06 → 2012-04 · intensity: 8
**Graduation and the Years That Preceded It**

I graduated with a BA in linguistics and a decade of experiences I hadn't had labels for yet: the difficulty sustaining attention in lecture, the surges of focus that were total, the projects I abandoned at eighty percent done because starting them had been the interesting part. I had a first-class result and no clear plan and a persistent sense that I was working around something structural rather than through it. Melbourne in 2011 was fine and I was twenty-two and I knew, without the words for it, that I'd been performing competence for long enough to be exhausted by the performance.

## E002 · 2012-02 → 2016-05 · intensity: 7
**The PhD: The Building and the Stalling**

I started the PhD on citation network topology — how linguistic papers cite each other, what the graph structure reveals about intellectual influence and status performance. I spent the first year building the data pipeline and the fourth year unable to revise the third chapter. Clancy pushed the timeline. I produced output and then produced nothing and then produced output again and the rhythm had no pattern I could understand. I finished. The thesis was good. I was thirty-two and I had a PhD in a field with approximately nine hundred jobs globally. I loved the work and didn't know whether the work loved me back enough to sustain a career.

## E003 · 2014-09 → 2015-06 · intensity: 8
**Meeting Yuki, Tokyo**

I was at a computational linguistics conference in Kyoto. Yuki was performing at a venue two blocks from my hotel — I followed a handmade sign on a lamppost. The music was made from recordings of train station announcements, deconstructed and reassembled into something that sounded like a language that had never existed. I went back the next night. We talked for three hours after the set on the fourth night about whether form or function preceded the other in language acquisition. They had opinions I hadn't considered. I had opinions they found useful. We exchanged contact details in a deliberate way that both of us knew was something more than professional. I flew back to Melbourne with a thesis chapter idea and Yuki's number and both of them scared me equally.

## E004 · 2018-03 → 2018-09 · intensity: 8
**The ADHD Diagnosis**

My GP referred me at thirty for evaluation, following a conversation about the thesis stalling in which I described my pattern of attention in more detail than I'd ever articulated to anyone. The psychologist who assessed me said the profile was clear and had probably been there since childhood. She said: does that explain things? I said: approximately everything. I sat in my car for forty minutes after the appointment. I cried once, briefly. The main feeling was relief with a layer of anger underneath it — not at anyone specific, but at the decade of managing symptoms I hadn't understood, and at the institutional systems that had rewarded my compensatory behaviors as if they were the natural thing and not the workaround.

## E005 · 2018-10 → 2019-06 · intensity: 7
**Methylphenidate, the First Year**

The medication worked immediately, which nobody had told me was common. The first week I finished three pieces of writing I'd been circling for months. I told Yuki over a video call and they said: you sound different. I said I feel like I found the settings menu. They said that was either very funny or a bit sad. We agreed it was both. The first year on medication was the most productive of my academic life and also the year I had to revise every story I'd told myself about why things had been hard. Some of those stories were simpler than I'd thought. Some were more interesting.

## E006 · 2019-06 → 2020-02 · intensity: 6
**The Citation Project Begins**

Sanjiv brought me the initial idea at a postdoc mixer: what if we formally tested the h-index manipulation pathways that everyone in the field knew existed but nobody had quantified? I said it would require crawling a significant portion of the Web of Science citation database. He said he'd thought about that. I said it would produce findings that a lot of powerful people would prefer not to exist. He said he'd thought about that too. I built the first data pipeline in three weeks. Clancy's office was two floors up. The project was the most technically interesting thing I'd started in years and also, I knew, the most structurally risky thing I'd started in my career.

## E007 · 2020-03 → 2020-12 · intensity: 7
**COVID and the Long Distance, Again**

Yuki's Tokyo circuit collapsed in March. Mine became Zoom conferences and a share house where one of my housemates had anxiety about shared surfaces and the other had anxiety about everything else. Yuki and I had been long-distance for three years and the pandemic added a border closure to the usual distance. We talked every other day and the conversations were the best of our relationship and also a kind of accumulated weight. I started learning Japanese on the video calls — Yuki would correct my pronunciation and I would correct their English apostrophes and we both found this more comforting than either of us could explain clearly.

## E008 · 2021-07 → 2022-03 · intensity: 8
**Clancy Reads the Preliminary Findings**

I gave Clancy a courtesy read of the preliminary findings in July 2021 — she had been my supervisor and the project touched her methodological legacy. She called me into her office two days later. The conversation lasted forty minutes. She told me the project was methodologically compromised, ethically questionable, and damaging to the department's reputation with the indexing organizations it depended on. I said the findings were real and the methodology was documented. She said the distinction between exposing a system and weaponizing the findings against it was not one she believed I'd made carefully. I left the meeting thinking she had identified a real question that didn't change what I was going to do. The fellowship renewal that was pending was not mentioned. It didn't need to be.

## E009 · 2022-06 → 2023-01 · intensity: 7
**The Fellowship Renewal: Conditional**

Clancy approved my renewal but changed the terms: the citation project was not to be named in my official research portfolio. I could conduct it privately but not under the university's institutional banner. I agreed in writing and then spent a week not telling Sanjiv. When I told him he said: conditional approval is how they launder complicity. I said he was probably right and that the alternative was losing the postdoc, which was the income I was using to run the project. He said he understood. The compromise sat in me as a compromise, not a resolution.

Yuki came for three months in February — the first extended time in the same city since before COVID. We were both aware that three months was a test as much as a visit. ## E011 · 2023-09 → 2024-04 · intensity: 7
**The Paper, the Preprint, the Response**

Sanjiv and I posted the preprint in September 2023. The response within the computational linguistics community was exactly what we'd modeled: half the field engaged with the methodology, a quarter criticized the framing, and a small but vocal group called for a formal retraction review before any peer-reviewed version appeared. One of the preprint critics was a former collaborator of Clancy. Anika told me the paper was methodologically solid and its institutional timing was maximally damaging. I said I'd tried to separate those two variables. She said I'd succeeded at one of them. That was a fair assessment.

## E013 · 2024-09 → 2025-04 · intensity: 5
**The Paper Published, the Silence**

The peer-reviewed version was accepted by a journal in the communication studies field rather than the computational linguistics field — we changed targets after two rejections that were clearly shaped by institutional interest rather than methodological objection. It published in November 2024. It received sustained but not spectacular attention. Clancy has not mentioned it. Anika said the field would absorb it slowly and it would matter in five years more than it matters now. I think she's right. I had expected more noise and got more silence, which in retrospect is the more effective outcome.
