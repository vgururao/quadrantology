## E001 · 1998-09 → 1999-07 · intensity: 8
**Alexandria to America**

I arrived in Washington DC for the Georgetown bioethics program at twenty-two. I had a medical degree and a set of experiences from Egyptian hospitals that had given me more clarity about what medicine could fail to protect than what it could save. The first semester was disorienting in the specific way that comes from realizing the framework you'd built your entire education around — in my case, the normative structures of Egyptian hospital ethics — was one possible framework among several competing ones, each with philosophical weight. I called my father from a Georgetown library payphone in November and said I wasn't sure I knew what ethics was anymore. He said: good. Now you can start.

## E002 · 2001-09-11 → 2001-11 · intensity: 9
**September, Hyde Park**

I was in the first year of my Johns Hopkins fellowship. The morning of the eleventh I was in a meeting about informed consent protocols that stopped when someone opened a laptop. I sat in that conference room until noon. For the following eight weeks I was stopped on the street twice and interrogated once in what someone told me afterward was a "voluntary" law enforcement interview. I carried my hospital ID and my fellowship documentation everywhere. I observed, from inside the experience, what it meant when an institution's commitment to principle was tested by an atmosphere of fear, and I watched some of my colleagues become carefully invisible and others become carefully loud. I have been thinking about that observation ever since.

## E003 · 2004-03 → 2005-01 · intensity: 7
**The Fellowship Ends, Northwestern Begins**

Northwestern Memorial hired me into the Clinical Ethics directorate in early 2004. I was twenty-eight and the youngest person in a leadership role in the department. The first eighteen months I spent learning that a hospital is a place where ethical principles operate in close proximity to financial pressures and that the proximity is not coincidental. Father Ibrahim, whom I met through the Chicago mosque that first year, said: you expected the complexity and you're still surprised by it. That was precisely right.

## E005 · 2012-04 → 2012-09 · intensity: 7
**The House, Buying Hyde Park**

We bought the house the year after the second back surgery turned out to be less definitive than the first. Hyde Park was the right neighborhood and the price was the right price and Yasmin handled most of the administrative process because I was in physiotherapy twice a week and the pain was still significant. I had not expected to own a house, which sounds like a small thing, but I had grown up in a rented apartment in Alexandria, moved through a succession of rented rooms from age twenty-two, and the permanence of a building with my name on the deed felt like a threshold I hadn't realized I was approaching. I sat on the front steps the evening we got the keys and thought about my parents and what they had built from less.

## E006 · 2015-04 → 2016-01 · intensity: 7
**The First Procedure Block**

I blocked a recommended procedure for the first time in my career — a high-margin spinal intervention on a patient who in my assessment had not given adequately informed consent and whose family had not been properly briefed on alternatives. Hendricks called me into his office. He said the clinical team was confident. I said confidence in the procedure wasn't the ethical question. He said I was substituting my judgment for the team's. I said I was exercising the function of the ethics directorate. He said I should be aware of the cost to the patient relationship. I said I was aware of the cost to the patient. The procedure was rescheduled with full consent. The patient chose a different intervention. Hendricks did not say anything more. Neither did I.

## E007 · 2017-08 → 2018-06 · intensity: 6
**The Second Surgery and What Changed**

The second back surgery was better than the first but required eight weeks of reduced capacity. I spent those weeks reading theology and philosophy in a way I hadn't had time for in years, and the time produced a clarity about the purpose of my clinical ethics function that I hadn't felt since the first years at Northwestern. Father Ibrahim visited twice. He told me that the body's limits are not limits on the spirit, which is not a thing I believe literally and is a thing I found genuinely useful. I came back to work narrower in terms of physical hours and sharper in terms of priority. Hendricks said I seemed different. I said I'd had time to think.

## E009 · 2020-03 → 2020-12 · intensity: 8
**COVID Ethics, the Ventilator Triage**

We needed a triage protocol for ventilator allocation in March 2020. I chaired the committee. We produced a protocol by April. Hendricks reviewed it, proposed an amendment that would have given preference to patients with larger insurance coverage under a "anticipated recovery and community contribution" framing, and I rejected the framing in writing. He said it was a value-neutral actuarial approach. I said it was not. The original protocol stood. I am aware that the original protocol also made terrible choices, because triage requires terrible choices. I made them using the framework I could defend. I believe this was correct and I think about it often.

## E011 · 2022-03 → 2022-09 · intensity: 6
**Ukraine, the Ethics of Aid Allocation**

The invasion prompted an internal ethics panel discussion about humanitarian obligations in resource allocation. I drafted a framework paper arguing that hospital systems in wealthy countries had a derivable moral obligation to the global injury burden in ways that the accreditation frameworks didn't require and the financial models didn't support. The paper circulated internally. Hendricks said it was interesting and outside our operational mandate. Elena thought the empirical framing was sound. Father Ibrahim read a copy I gave him and said it was the most useful thing I'd written since the COVID triage protocol, and that it would have no effect and should be written anyway.

## E012 · 2023-07 → 2024-03 · intensity: 7
**The Major Procedure Block and the Formal Complaint**

A medical device manufacturer had been running a research protocol at Northwestern for two years. I reviewed it in July 2023 and concluded that the consent documentation was inadequate and the conflict of interest disclosure was incomplete. I halted the protocol pending revision. Hendricks escalated. The manufacturer filed a formal complaint with the hospital board. I submitted my analysis to the board directly. The board reviewed over three months. The protocol was revised and the block was upheld. Hendricks said the relationship with the manufacturer was now damaged. I said the alternative was continuing a protocol with inadequate consent. He said I was aware of the cost. I said I was.

## E013 · 2024-04 → 2024-10 · intensity: 5
**Samir's Visit**

My brother flew from Cairo in April. He runs a private cardiology practice. We spent three evenings arguing about the Egyptian healthcare system, which he operates inside with pragmatic acceptance, and the American system, which I fight inside with principled refusal, and whether either of us was making a different kind of peace. He said I had the luxury of principles because the American system had sufficient slack to absorb them. I said the Egyptian system had sufficient corruption to make pragmatism necessary. We reached no conclusion. We had excellent food. He asked after our mother on behalf of both of us, which is what we do when we've argued enough.
