## E001 · 2003-09 → 2004-06 · intensity: 8
**Vienna and the Leaving**

I left Kyiv at twenty-one on an exchange programme and stayed. The decision wasn't a single moment but a series of smaller ones — the WU enrollment, the part-time work permit, the phone call home where I told my mother I wasn't coming back for the semester I'd said. Olena said: you'll come back for the summer. I said: yes. I came back for the summer and then enrolled in the autumn again. Eventually the direction was clear and we both stopped negotiating the timeline. I loved Kyiv and I didn't want to live there. That gap has stayed with me as a private, complicated thing I don't fully explain to Austrian friends who ask about Ukraine.

## E002 · 2005-06 → 2007-03 · intensity: 7
**The MSc and the First Build**

I finished the WU master's and immediately started a software company with a university friend who had good ideas and no discipline. The company failed in eighteen months. The product worked. The operations didn't, and that was my fault as much as his. I was better at building than at running. I learned that distinctly, not as a general observation but as a specific thing that happened when I tried to manage our first hire and discovered I had no vocabulary for the management part. The failure was clean and complete and I took eighteen months of lessons from it that I applied, imperfectly, to the next three attempts.

## E003 · 2009-04 → 2010-02 · intensity: 8
**Meeting Felix, the Argument That Didn't End**

Felix was in the room at a startup pitch event, pitching something adjacent to what I was building. Our ideas overlapped enough that we competed for the same angel investor. The investor chose me. Felix came up afterward and said I'd pitched the right problem with the wrong solution. I told him his solution was right and his market read was wrong. We argued for an hour and a half in a café while the event finished around us. We started dating two months later. We co-founded Refract three years after that. The argument has continued, in various productive forms, for fifteen years. Neither of us has been cleanly right or cleanly wrong about anything major.

## E005 · 2015-03 → 2016-06 · intensity: 6
**The First Successful Exit**

We sold a company — a different one, my second attempt before Refract — in early 2015. The exit was small: €400,000 total, split two ways. More than I'd ever had in one place. I put €200k into savings and started thinking about Refract. Felix said: finally a real problem. I said: it was always a real problem. He said: no, the other one was a solution looking for a problem. He was probably right. The exit money was the seed capital that gave us two years of runway on Refract. The two years ran out and we raised, and the company is still running, which is more than I expected and less than I needed it to be by now.

## E006 · 2018-09 → 2019-07 · intensity: 7
**The Third Pivot, Dieter's Warning**

Refract pivoted for the third time in 2018. I was proud of the pivot. Dieter, our lead investor, was not. He came to a board meeting and said, in the careful language of a man who has seen this before and is giving a final-warning speech, that the company's value to investors was its product consistency and that consistent pivoting was the opposite of a product. Felix argued with him. I said we understood. After the meeting Felix said I'd been too deferential. I said I'd been strategic. We argued about that specific call for approximately six hours over two weeks. I still think I was right. I'm also not sure the distinction mattered.

## E007 · 2022-02 → 2022-07 · intensity: 9
**The Invasion**

I was on a call with a Berlin client when the news came. My mother had called six times in the previous hour and I hadn't seen it. When I reached Olena she said: they're bombing. She was in Kyiv. She had decided to stay, which was the decision she had made clearly and which I had not fully believed she would actually make until the bombs were falling. I spent the next two days trying to reach her, watching the news, and sitting in my Vienna office with a phone and a laptop and the specific uselessness of being a founder during a crisis that has nothing to do with your company. She was fine. She has remained fine. I call twice a week. The calls are the most important hours of my week and the most frightening ones.

## E008 · 2022-03 → 2022-10 · intensity: 8
**Russian Off Everything**

I stopped using Russian in professional contexts in March 2022. I stopped using it socially a month later. I switched the language settings on every device. My Russian is native-fluent; it was the language of my secondary school education and of half my earliest technical reading. Stripping it out of daily use felt simultaneously like an act of principle and like losing a room of my mind. I don't regret it. I don't think it changed anything externally. It changed something internally that I needed to change and I can't explain more precisely than that.

## E011 · 2024-01 → 2024-07 · intensity: 5
**Olena's Visit**

My mother came to Vienna in the spring for the first time since 2021. She arrived thinner and more alert than I remembered, which is what two years of wartime does. She saw Katya and cried in the kitchen in a way I hadn't seen her cry before, not from sadness but from something I can only describe as relief at the specific form of continuity that is a grandchild who looks like you. They talked for three hours in Ukrainian and I sat outside and listened through the window. The visit lasted two weeks. When she left I stood on the street for a long time. Vienna was ordinary and impossibly intact.
