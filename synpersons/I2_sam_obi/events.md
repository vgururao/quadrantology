## E001 · 2015-07 → 2016-03 · intensity: 8
**UNILAG Graduation, Lagos Arrives**

I graduated and moved from Enugu to Lagos on a three-month sub-let and a verbal offer from a community newspaper that fell through a week after I arrived. I spent four months writing for free — an environmental story about a waste dump in Ajegunle, a housing piece about a Yaba landlord collective — until Premium Times ran a freelance piece and Ngozi Obi called me to ask where I'd been. I said I'd been trying to be the kind of journalist Premium Times would want to hire. She said: come in Monday. Lagos arrived all at once that summer — the density, the speed, the specific sense that if you couldn't find the story in this city you couldn't find it anywhere.

## E002 · 2017-02 → 2017-10 · intensity: 7
**The First Significant Investigation**

My first major investigation took nine months. A pharmaceutical distribution company was selling counterfeit malaria medications in rural markets in Enugu and Anambra states. I documented the supply chain by tracking batch numbers backward through three intermediary distributors to a Lagos warehouse that had no pharmaceutical license. Ngozi held the story for six weeks after I filed it, reviewing the documentation with the legal team. It ran in October. The company was investigated by NAFDAC. Two distributors were arrested. I went to my mother's church in Enugu on Christmas that year and sat in the pew thinking about the malaria patients who wouldn't get fake drugs from that particular warehouse anymore, and whether that was enough, and knowing that it was the starting point.

## E003 · 2019-03 → 2019-09 · intensity: 8
**The Abuja Procurement Story, The Threat**

I broke a story about fraudulent infrastructure procurement contracts in Abuja that named two contractors and a civil servant. One of the contractors called the Premium Times offices and left a specific message about my travel safety. Ngozi called me immediately and we agreed I would not travel to Abuja alone for six months. I told my mother I was working from Lagos for a while. I didn't tell her why. The story produced three follow-up stories from other outlets and a legislative committee inquiry that resulted in one of the contractors being debarred. The debarment felt inadequate. The story was the foundation of a methodology I've used eight times since.

The Yaba Press FC Saturday football stopped in March. I noticed this more than I expected to. My mother called weekly and sent food parcels that arrived mostly on time. I played indoor table tennis in my flat by myself, which is a depressing activity I do not recommend, and restarted when the football came back in August.

## E005 · 2021-04 → 2021-10 · intensity: 7
**The Investigation Begins**

Chief Eze made first contact in April. He was a former government contractor who had documentation about a procurement irregularity at the federal level — the kind of irregularity that involved names that made Ngozi's first response careful and specific: "If this is what it looks like, you need more than documents." I spent six months building the evidentiary framework: cross-referencing Eze's documents with published contract records, company filings, and three independent sources who confirmed pieces of the pattern without knowing about each other. By October I had a story I believed in and a source I needed to protect and a legal analysis from Chidi Okoye that I read three times and set aside.

## E006 · 2021-11 → 2022-07 · intensity: 7
**Kelechi's Warning and What I Said**

Kelechi called in November 2021, which was unusual enough that I knew the call was about the story before she said anything. She'd seen enough financial forensics to recognize what I was working on from the document types I'd mentioned. She said: Sam, this sounds like it could go badly. I said: I know. She said: I mean for you specifically, not for the story. I said: I know. She said: okay. And then we talked about our mother for twenty minutes and I didn't tell Kelechi that what she'd said had kept me awake for three nights. It still comes back. I've decided it's useful information rather than a deterrent.

## E007 · 2022-09 → 2023-06 · intensity: 6
**The Dollar Account**

I opened a USD savings account in 2022. The naira had been losing real value for years and the Premium Times salary, paid in naira, was eroding in purchasing power in a way I could calculate precisely. I set aside $200 per month. It required discipline I found easier to maintain than I'd expected, because the specific alternative — watching the naira number in the local account stay the same while the rent went up — was clarifying. By mid-2023 I had $3,800 in the USD account, which is a small amount by any objective measure and a significant resource in the context of Lagos journalism. Ngozi says she doesn't know how I do it on the salary. I don't tell her about the currency hedging.

## E008 · 2023-03 → 2023-11 · intensity: 8
**The Story Closes, The Risk Sharpens**

By March 2023 the evidentiary package was essentially complete: fourteen months of documentation, four independent source confirmations, company registry analysis, and a forensic accounting review Kelechi helped me structure. Eze was named as a source in the internal filing notes, which Ngozi saw. She called it the most solid investigation the paper had published in five years. She also said she was not yet ready to call it publishable and that she was measuring a different variable than solidity. I understood the variable. I did not agree with the calibration. We had the same conversation four more times between March and November.

## E009 · 2023-10 → 2024-05 · intensity: 7
**Eze's Exposure**

Eze called me in October 2023. He'd been approached by someone who knew he was a source — not from the paper, but through a business contact who'd asked specific questions about his document history. He didn't know if it was connected to the investigation. I don't know either. I advised him on operational security measures and escalated to Ngozi the same day. Ngozi said: this changes the publication timeline. I said: it means we can't wait indefinitely. She said: I know. We agreed on a contingent publication plan — a trigger condition under which the story would run regardless of whether we'd completed every line of verification. I wrote the trigger condition in a memo I keep off the main server.

## E010 · 2024-01 → 2024-08 · intensity: 5
**The Football Group**

Yaba Press FC turned fifteen years old in January 2024. We are not the same people who started the group; about a third of the original regulars have left Lagos or stopped coming. I'm one of the longer-serving members now. We played a tournament against two other informal groups in March and lost both games. I scored a goal in the second game that was disallowed for offside, which I maintain was wrong, and the referee's call has not changed. The group is the most unconditional relationship in my Lagos life — no sources, no editors, no story. Just football and dust.

## E011 · 2024-06 → 2025-01 · intensity: 6
**The Malaria Papers**

I published a secondary investigation in June 2024 — not the main story, a separate piece about malaria drug quality in northern markets that I'd been building on the side for two years. It was the cleanest investigation I'd run since the pharmaceutical piece in 2017 and it ran without complications. I went to Enugu for my mother's birthday in August and she had read the piece and introduced me to her friend's son as the person who had written about the drugs. The friend's son said his sister had been sick last year and asked whether the drugs he'd bought were among the ones I'd written about. I gave him the brand names and the batch numbers to check. That's the thing you can do.

## E012 · 2025-02 → 2025-08 · intensity: 7

## E013 · 2025-04 → 2025-10 · intensity: 8
**The Aftermath**

The story produced immediate institutional responses: a statement from the ministry, a denial from one contractor, silence from two others, and a Lagos state assembly motion for an inquiry. Premium Times received two formal legal notices within ten days. Ngozi managed both. One was withdrawn after legal review. The other is still active. I have not received personal communications related to the story, which is either reassuring or a different kind of information. Kelechi called the morning the story ran. She didn't say anything for a moment and then said: you did it right. That's the sentence I've been sitting with.

## E014 · 2025-11 → 2026-02 · intensity: 5
**My Mother's Call**

My mother called in November, after the story had been in the public record for seven months and the inquiry was ongoing. She asked how I was. I said I was fine. She asked whether the work was safe now. I said: safer than before. She said: that's not the same as safe. I said: I know.
