## E001 · 1995-06 → 1996-09 · intensity: 8
**Bergen to Oslo, First Principles**

I finished my law degree in Oslo and understood almost immediately that the cases I wanted to take were not the cases that made money. My mother — who had spent thirty years on the bench watching the rule of law fail when lawyers refused to defend unpopular cases — said: the principle is content-neutral and so must be the lawyer. I had heard this my entire childhood and understood it abstractly. The first time I defended it personally was a 1995 case — a neo-Nazi publication fighting a government seizure order. I was fresh-certified. I took the case on procedure alone: the seizure had been conducted without a proper court order. I won on procedure. I was twenty-five and the hate mail was my first and it clarified exactly what my mother had been telling me.

## E002 · 1997-09 → 1998-06 · intensity: 7
**Columbia, the American Courts**

The Columbia LLM was the sharpest year of my education. The American constitutional tradition has a different relationship to free expression than the Norwegian one — more absolute in principle, more contested in practice. I wrote a comparative analysis of the Norwegian Grunnloven Article 100 and the First Amendment that my supervisor called "a useful corrective from outside the tradition." I found American constitutional litigation simultaneously more principled and more performative than Norwegian practice. The performance bothered me. I came home knowing what I didn't want the Norwegian practice to become.

## E003 · 2001-03 → 2002-01 · intensity: 8
**The First Depression**

I was thirty-one and the partner track at the Oslo firm had been confirmed and the cases were the right cases and I couldn't get out of bed some mornings. I didn't have the vocabulary for it. I called it overwork. My doctor called it a depressive episode and said it was treatable. The medication and the work it required to function through the first treatment period were the most difficult things I'd done to that point. Petter Tvedt — we were associates together at the time — covered three client meetings for me without being told why. He said afterward he'd seen what he'd seen and it was between us. That offer of discreet competence is a large part of why I eventually asked him to co-found the firm.

## E004 · 2006-09 → 2007-06 · intensity: 7
**The Firm Opens, the Cost**

Larsen & Tvedt opened in September 2006. I was thirty-six. Erik was two years old. The timing was impossible and I made it work anyway, which is the thing my son has watched me do his entire life and which has given him a complicated relationship with what "work" means in a life well-lived. He moved to Berlin at twenty-three and I miss him more than I've told him. I said the firm was good. I meant it. The cost was the years when Erik needed more of me than the cases allowed.

## E005 · 2009-11 → 2010-08 · intensity: 6
**The Surveillance Case**

I took a challenge to the Norwegian Police Security Service's bulk surveillance program in 2009 — the first of three cases I've run in that area. The legal terrain was new and technically complex and Petter was skeptical of the resource commitment for an uncertain outcome. We won on a narrow procedural point that required the service to provide more specific justification for its data collection request in that particular case. The press called it a landmark. I called it a fact-specific ruling that would require three follow-on cases to have structural effect. I was right. I brought the follow-on cases.

## E006 · 2012-07 → 2013-04 · intensity: 7
**The Second Episode**

The second depressive episode hit in the summer. I recognized it faster this time and adjusted the medication and the schedule. I told Petter directly — not the details, but enough. He said: what do you need from me? I said: six weeks where you handle the client communications without involving me. He said: done. The six weeks held. I came back at full capacity. The management of the condition has been stable on escitalopram since 2008 and the stability has been earned rather than given, which is a distinction that matters to me. I've talked about it publicly since 2015. This is the most political thing I've done, possibly.

He and I are too similar in one specific way — the capacity for solitude that looks like independence and is also partly withdrawal. I worried he'd inherited the depression. ## E008 · 2019-03 → 2019-11 · intensity: 7
**The Journalist Client**

Aminata Kouyaté came to us through a referral from an international press freedom organization. She was facing prosecution under a provision of Norwegian law that had not been tested against the ECHR's press freedom standards in the specific way her case required. The legal argument was strong. Aminata's instinct was to hold press conferences. I stopped two, redirected a third into a formal statement coordinated with the legal strategy, and spent eight months explaining to a committed journalist why the procedural constraints were protecting her rather than silencing her. She said she understood. She held a press conference anyway in October. I attended to limit the damage. We are still working together and I still like her.

## E009 · 2020-03 → 2020-12 · intensity: 6
**The Pandemic, Birthe's Letters**

My mother began writing letters in March 2020 — actual paper letters, because the pandemic had given her time and because she'd never trusted video calls for conversations that mattered. She wrote about her cases, about the Bergen winters, about my father who had died in 2012. She wrote one letter specifically about whether a state of emergency justified restrictions on civil liberties and concluded it did not, with citations. She was eighty at the time. I wrote back. We had the most substantive correspondence of our adult relationship during the lockdown months. I kept the letters. Birthe is still sharp and still critical.

## E010 · 2021-06 → 2022-02 · intensity: 7
**The Aminata Verdict**

The Oslo City Court found in favor of Aminata in June 2021. The judgment was a significant clarification of ECHR Article 10 application in Norwegian press freedom cases. I wrote the press statement. Aminata held a press conference that was more effusive than I'd have preferred and precisely as effective as I'd hoped. Per Halvorsen, opposing counsel, sent a two-line note: "Correct outcome. Good argument. Per." He's represented the state in nine of my cases. I value those notes more than most of the tributes I receive from people on my side.

## E011 · 2022-09 → 2023-06 · intensity: 5
**The Frogner Bath, Fifteen Years**

I've been swimming at the Frogner Baths three mornings a week for fifteen years. The routine is the structural support for everything else. I've calculated that I've swum approximately 4,400 kilometers in that time, which is Oslo to Istanbul and back roughly. I haven't told anyone this calculation except Petter, who said: that's either the most legal or the most obsessive thing you've ever told me. I said: it's the same thing. He laughed. The swimming began as stress management and has become something I can't evaluate from outside because it's part of the system that does the evaluating.

## E012 · 2023-07 → 2024-03 · intensity: 7
**The New Surveillance Case**

I opened a third surveillance challenge in July 2023 — the technology had changed significantly since the 2009 case and the statutory framework had acquired provisions that required reexamination under updated ECHR guidance. Petter reviewed the resource estimate and said it was the most expensive case we'd ever opened and that it was the right case to open. That combination of assessments, in that order, is the most useful thing Petter offers me. The case is in the preliminary documentation phase. It will take four years. I've cleared the calendar.

## E014 · 2024-10 → 2025-06 · intensity: 6
**Birthe, the Visit**

My mother's health declined through the autumn. Nothing acute — a series of smaller reductions in what she could manage independently. I drove to Bergen in November and spent four days. She asked about the surveillance case. She asked about Aminata. She asked about Erik. She asked whether I was still swimming. In the order of those questions — the work first, the people second, the self last — I heard myself. I told her she was the origin of every principle I'd argued in court. She said: I know, but I want to hear you say it. So I said it.
