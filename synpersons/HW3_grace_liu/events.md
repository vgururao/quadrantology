## E001 · 2016-05 → 2016-09 · intensity: 8
**UCLA Graduation, The Waitress Summer**

I graduated with a sociology degree and went back to Flushing to waitress at my mother's dry-cleaning customer's restaurant while I figured out what organizing actually meant versus what I'd been told it meant in class. The restaurant was owned by a family whose cleaning bills my mother had extended credit on twice during slow months. I thought about that relationship all summer — the informal credit, the reciprocal loyalty, the system of mutual support my mother had built through twenty years of careful relationships within a community that the formal economy had not built anything for. I left for LA in September thinking I understood the gap between theory and practice. I had not begun to understand it.

## E002 · 2016-11 → 2017-06 · intensity: 7
**The Election and the Campaign That Followed**

I watched the 2016 results in a bar in Koreatown with six other people I barely knew. The morning after I sent an email to the Workers Alliance about the canvassing program I'd seen advertised. Martha Fuentes interviewed me in December. She said: why do you want to do this? I said: because the alternative is being someone who watches things get worse. She said: that's a reason to be angry. I need to know if you can organize. I said: I can learn. She said: that's the right answer. She hired me in January. I spent the first six months doing voter registration tables in strip mall parking lots in temperatures above 90 degrees and thinking this was approximately the most useful thing I had ever done.

## E003 · 2018-04 → 2019-02 · intensity: 8
**The Warehouse Campaign, First Win**

We ran a successful organizing campaign at a warehouse facility in the eastern San Fernando Valley. Three hundred and forty workers voted to authorize union representation. I was the lead organizer for the final push. Martha called it one of the cleanest campaigns she'd seen in a decade. I went home that night and cried from something that wasn't exactly joy and wasn't exactly relief — more like the specific relief of having done a hard thing correctly for the right reasons. I called my mother. She said she was glad it went well. She asked if this meant I had a stable job now. I said it meant the workers had a union. She said: yes, but what about you?

## E004 · 2020-03 → 2020-09 · intensity: 7
**COVID, Essential Workers, the Shift**

COVID hit the worker communities we organized harder than the city was prepared to admit. I spent four months in the pandemic's first wave running emergency support — food access, emergency childcare referrals, workers' compensation cases for people who were being told their exposure was not work-related. Ray and I moved in together in April because it made logistical sense during the lockdown. The domestic proximity was good and clarifying: it showed me what I'd already been noticing — that Ray understood my politics intellectually and didn't understand my level of commitment personally. We didn't name that during the lockdown. We had other things to manage.

## E005 · 2021-01 → 2021-09 · intensity: 6
**The UCLA Law School Decision**

Martha said I needed the JD to fight the company lawyers on their own terrain. I'd been resisting it for four years because law school felt like an institutional concession. Daniel Park changed my mind at a coffee with three sentences about what environmental justice activists had accomplished through environmental law in the nineties. He said the tool isn't the ideology. I thought about that. I enrolled in September. The first semester was the most intellectually rigorous and personally irritating experience I'd had since organizing school — rigorous because the analytical frameworks were useful and irritating because half my classmates planned to take the frameworks and use them for corporate clients.

## E006 · 2021-10 → 2022-07 · intensity: 7
**The Campaign That Led to the Lawsuit**

We ran an aggressive public campaign targeting a logistics company's labour practices — social media, coalition letters, worker testimony at city council. The company's legal team filed a tortious interference lawsuit against the Alliance in April 2022. Martha said: they're intimidated. Our attorney said: they have a case, partially. I spent three months learning the specific legal theory under which campaign tactics I believed were protected speech could constitute interference with commercial relationships. The lawsuit didn't kill the campaign. It complicated it in ways that changed how I thought about tactics. Daniel Park's voice in my head got louder.

## E007 · 2022-09 → 2023-05 · intensity: 7
**Ray Leaves**

Ray told me in September that he thought we wanted different things. He meant it carefully: not that he didn't believe in the work, but that he was realizing he wanted a life with someone whose work was more adjacent to his, less consuming, less structurally incompatible with the domestic rhythms he'd imagined for himself. I had known this was coming for longer than I'd been willing to say. The conversation was honest and sad and I appreciated his honesty. I moved into the Koreatown one-bedroom in October. I was twenty-eight, alone, in debt from law school, in a lawsuit, and more certain about the work than I'd been since the warehouse vote. The certainty and the loneliness were both real.

## E008 · 2023-04 → 2023-10 · intensity: 6
**The Campaigns Director Role**

Martha stepped back from day-to-day operations in the spring. She asked me to take the director role. I was twenty-eight and the youngest campaigns director the Alliance had employed. I said yes and spent the first three months learning that managing a campaign team required a completely different set of skills than running a campaign. I was worse at the management than the organizing, which I had not expected. Daniel told me that the people who were good at the work weren't always good at teaching others to do the work, and that the director role required the latter. I was annoyed by this and it was correct.

## E009 · 2023-10 → 2024-06 · intensity: 5
**My Mother's Pipes**

The dry-cleaning building's plumbing failed in October and my mother spent four months dealing with contractors who wanted more money than the lease required and a landlord who contested the lease language. She had an informal network of other business owners on the block who'd seen it before, and they helped her through it, and she came out of it with a settled landlord relationship and a new lease clause. I helped her review the clause. She said: see, the law is useful. She said it in a way that suggested she'd been waiting for me to be useful in a specific way for a while. I said: I've been useful in other ways. She said: I know, but this kind I can explain to people.

## E010 · 2024-01 → 2024-07 · intensity: 6
**The Lawsuit Settles**

The tortious interference lawsuit settled in January 2024. The settlement required us to modify two specific tactics we'd used in the campaign. Our attorney said the settlement was a win on the terms that mattered. Martha agreed. I spent a month deciding whether the tactic modifications represented a principled compromise or a capitulation. Daniel said: the question isn't whether you changed tactics. The question is whether you can still run the campaign with what remains. We could. I accepted this and kept some of the discomfort anyway, because I think the discomfort is useful.

## E011 · 2024-04 → 2024-12 · intensity: 5
**The JD Progress, the Exhaustion**

I'm in the third year of a four-year part-time JD and the exhaustion is the dominant texture of my current life. I work full-time, attend class three nights a week, and read on weekends. Daniel makes the reading useful — he's in the same program and we've become genuine study partners. He argues from proceduralism, I argue from outcome, and we've reached a mutual respect for the gap between us that I didn't expect when we started. I sleep six hours a night and exercise when I can. I am not healthy. I am less unhealthy than last year.

## E012 · 2025-02 → 2025-08 · intensity: 6
**The Big Campaign Launch**

We launched the campaign I've been building for three years: a coordinated push for wage theft enforcement in the gig economy sector in Los Angeles. The legal strategy, the communications strategy, and the worker organizing components are the most integrated work the Alliance has produced. Martha, now advisory, called it the best opening I'd run. Brian Walsh's PR team issued a holding statement within forty-eight hours, which means they'd been watching. I took that as evidence the campaign was the right target. The first three months are the hardest.

## E013 · 2025-04 → 2025-10 · intensity: 5
**Mother's Birthday, Flushing**

I flew to Flushing for my mother's sixtieth birthday. She had requested a specific restaurant and invited twelve people. I sat at that table and watched her manage a dinner with the same focused operational competence she brings to every situation where she's responsible for people in her care. I thought about the distance between how I'd framed her choices when I was twenty-two — as a survival strategy I needed to transcend — and how I understood them now. She didn't build the system I'm fighting. She navigated it to keep her family fed and her business running for thirty years. Those aren't the same thing. I owe her a more honest conversation about that gap.

## E015 · 2026-03 → 2026-04 · intensity: 6
**The Campaign Breakthrough**

The Los Angeles City Council voted in March to strengthen gig worker wage theft enforcement with an expanded enforcement mechanism we'd been lobbying for. It wasn't everything we'd asked for. It was real. I called Martha. She said: this is what winning looks like. It looks like more work. I said: I know. I called my mother. She said she'd seen it in the news and she was proud and when was I coming home. I said next month. She said good, she needed help with a new lease clause.
