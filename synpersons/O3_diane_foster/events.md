## E001 · 2002-05 → 2003-04 · intensity: 8
**Spelman Graduation, The Choice That Wasn't**

I graduated Spelman and had two paths: a consulting offer in Atlanta at $52,000 a year or a fellowship at a Memphis housing nonprofit at $24,000. My grandmother, who had lived in Memphis her whole life and who did not understand consulting at all, said: you can always make money later. The fellowship turned out to be the most important two years of my education — not because the nonprofit was well-run (it wasn't) but because I learned, from the inside, every failure mode a community organization can have and still persist. I've been running the playbook of what not to do ever since, which sounds negative and is one of the most useful things I know.

## E002 · 2004-09 → 2006-05 · intensity: 7
**Harvard Kennedy School and the Program Design Work**

The Kennedy School gave me frameworks for the intuitions I'd built in Memphis. The most important course was program evaluation — how to know if something is working, which turns out to be a harder question than most program designers ask before they spend the money. My thesis was on capital allocation in community development finance, which is the kind of abstract description that covered a very specific question: why do the programs that work never have enough money and the ones that don't keep getting funded? I left with an answer I still think is right and a set of relationships that have determined the arc of my career.

## E003 · 2008-05 → 2009-03 · intensity: 7
**The Financial Crisis and the Programs**

The 2008 crisis hit the families on New Roots' waiting lists in waves: first the housing, then the employment, then the food access, then the mental health. I was a program director at the time, not yet executive director. I watched the executive director make decisions in the crisis and I started keeping notes on what I would do differently — not out of ambition but out of the specific frustration of watching correct analysis produce insufficient response because the organizational capacity wasn't there. Three years later I was running the organization. I've used those notes twice.

## E005 · 2016-11 → 2017-06 · intensity: 6
**The Election, the Funder Call**

Two of our major funders called in November 2016 to ask how the election might affect our program areas. The question was real — the federal housing and food assistance programs we built on were politically exposed. I gave both of them an accurate assessment of the risk, a contingency scenario, and a modified budget request. Both funders renewed. The program survived a period that eliminated four comparable organizations in Atlanta. The survival was built on the assessment I'd given: here is the risk, here is the plan, here is what you can do. The honesty was the strategy.

## E006 · 2019-03 → 2019-11 · intensity: 7
**Keisha Brown's First Year**

Keisha came in March as housing program director. By November I was actively managing the relationship between her advocacy instincts and the organizational boundaries those instincts kept crossing. She had confronted a city housing official publicly at a community meeting in a way that was correct on substance and cost the program a permit expediting relationship we'd been building for two years. I told her what she'd cost and why. She said: those people needed to hear it. I said: they heard it and the permit is now delayed four months. She said: I can live with that. I said: the families on the list also live with it. She didn't say anything after that. Neither did I. That conversation has been the template for every one we've had since.

## E007 · 2020-03 → 2020-09 · intensity: 7
**COVID, the Emergency Programming**

I ran four emergency programs simultaneously from March through September: emergency food boxes, rental assistance for eviction-prevented households, internet access grants for distance learning, and a job placement matching service for essential workers. The programs operated on emergency funding with informal governance and produced results I've been trying to institutionalize since. The moment I remember most from the summer was sitting in the parking lot of the grant warehouse at 11 p.m. on a Tuesday and calling Dr. Pamela Reid and saying: I think I'm operating correctly and I'm not sure I'm okay. She said: those are separate questions and I'm glad you can tell them apart. That was the right answer.

**Gerald Mason's Opinions**

Gerald Mason increased his annual gift to $400,000 in September 2020 and began calling monthly with program design opinions. Some were useful. Most were adjacent to useful in the way that a donor with resources and context but not operational experience is frequently adjacent to useful. **The Apartment Question**

I've been renting in Atlanta for eight years. I ran the affordability calculation in April 2022 for the fourth time in five years. The result was the same: on an executive director's salary in the current Atlanta market, ownership isn't achievable without either a second income or a family wealth transfer I don't have. ## E014 · 2025-11 → 2026-03 · intensity: 4
**The Waiting List Numbers**

We have 340 families on the housing program waiting list as of February 2026. This number has never been below 200 in the twelve years I've been running New Roots. It goes up in recessions and natural disasters and when the rental market does what the Atlanta rental market has done. It goes down when we add program capacity and then goes back up. I report the number at every board meeting. Richard Park asks about the methodology. I explain the methodology. The number is the number. It's the most honest thing in my quarterly report.
