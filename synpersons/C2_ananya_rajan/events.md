## E001 · 1994-09 → 1995-06 · intensity: 8
**MIT Arrives Like a Wall**

I came from Delhi University top of my year, and MIT arrived like a wall I hadn't seen. The first semester I spent understanding that being the smartest person in the room had been a fact about Delhi, not about me. My advisor told me, not unkindly, that my undergraduate training had given me good answers and not yet good questions. I didn't sleep well for eight months. I learned to ask questions by watching the people around me fail at them without embarrassment, and slowly I understood that the willingness to be visibly confused was the actual skill no one in Delhi had taught me.

## E002 · 1998-11 → 1999-06 · intensity: 8
**The Dissertation, The Doubt**

I defended in November 1998 and walked out of the committee room thinking the entire argument was wrong. Not the conclusion — the methodology. Three of my five chapters rested on an assumption about capital flow symmetry that I had never tested directly because everyone assumed it was true. I published it anyway because that's what you do when you've spent six years on something. For two years afterward I kept waiting for someone to catch it. No one did, which was worse than if they had.

## E003 · 2003-09 → 2005-02 · intensity: 7
**The Euro Argument, and Being Right**

I published a paper in 2003 arguing the eurozone's design would create asymmetric shocks that it had no institutional capacity to absorb. My colleagues at LSE thought it was theoretically interesting and empirically premature. Oliver Stanton wrote a referee report so dismissive I still have it filed under "motivation." When the Greek crisis hit in 2010, six people sent me the paper. The vindication didn't feel the way I had imagined. I'd spent seven years being told I was overclaiming, and being right felt mostly like relief rather than triumph, and the relief was quickly replaced by the awareness that being right hadn't changed anything structural.

## E004 · 2008-09 → 2009-06 · intensity: 7
**The Crash, The Recession Call I Got Wrong**

I predicted a US recession timed for Q4 2009. I was early by six months on one measure and wrong by eighteen months on another, depending on which data you used. At a conference in Brussels I said aloud that the recovery was beginning, and then the recovery stalled for another year. Stanton cited this in print. I spent three months feeling what I can only describe as hot, as if embarrassment had a temperature. I corrected the record in a note in the same journal and the correction received a fifth of the attention the original call had. That asymmetry is the thing about this profession nobody tells you honestly.

## E005 · 2011-03 → 2012-01 · intensity: 6
**Ravi and the NGO Years**

Ravi took the London development job in 2011, which was the right move for him and a minor domestic upheaval for us. He traveled four months a year and I was running the LSE term and supervising three doctoral students and we both did the thing where you don't fight about the logistics directly because fighting about the logistics is just fighting about the deeper question of whose work matters more. We didn't answer the deeper question. We just adjusted the logistics until they worked well enough, and then we stopped noticing how hard we'd adjusted.

## E006 · 2015-06 → 2016-03 · intensity: 5
**Arjun Moves to San Francisco**

He'd been offered a role at a machine learning lab and I helped him with the visa paperwork, which is a strange way to help someone leave. He called from his first flat in the Mission District and described the view and I listened and thought about what it means to raise a child to be capable of complete independence and then to feel the independence as something else when it arrives. He still reads my papers. He argues with them by email now instead of in the kitchen. I prefer the kitchen version but this one is fine too.

## E007 · 2016-06 → 2016-08 · intensity: 6
**Brexit Morning**

I had been writing about currency risk in the UK for two years, but the morning of the result I sat in the kitchen and cried. Not about the economics. About the tone of it, the specific ugliness of what had won. Ravi made tea and didn't try to fix it with an argument. I had a piece due by noon for the Guardian and I wrote it and it was accurate and I hated writing it. That afternoon I gave a department seminar that had been scheduled months before and I stood in front of twenty people and tried to be analytically useful about something that had made me sad, and I think I succeeded at the analytic part and no one asked about the other part.

## E008 · 2019-02 → 2019-05 · intensity: 6
**IMF Consulting and the Washington Feeling**

They asked me to consult on a debt sustainability framework review, which sounds neutral and is not. Washington has a register for conversations — you say things obliquely there, in a way that lets everyone pretend the disagreements aren't disagreements. I gave three workshops where I said clearly what I thought and watched the room absorb it politely and then revert to the prior consensus in the discussion session. On the flight home I thought about the difference between having influence and having access. I had been confused about which one I was being offered.

## E009 · 2020-03 → 2020-09 · intensity: 7
**Lockdown, the Diabetes, and Working from the Kitchen**

The lockdown arrived the same week my GP told me my blood glucose was in the pre-diabetic range. Ravi and I were suddenly in the flat together every day for the first time since we'd both had careers. We argued, constructively and non-constructively, about macroeconomic policy, about whether I was eating enough vegetables, about his NGO's pivot to remote programming. I did three months of intensive diet work and brought the A1C to 6.4, which I found more satisfying than any journal acceptance that year. The diabetes was the most honest data I'd received about myself in a decade.

## E010 · 2020-06 → 2021-02 · intensity: 5
**The Covid Recession Paper**

I published a piece in the Economic Journal arguing that the pandemic would produce a K-shaped recovery that standard distributional models would miss. The piece was well-received in circles that already agreed with me. The circles that didn't weren't reading it. I presented it at three virtual conferences and the Zoom format produced a specific exhaustion I hadn't expected: the inability to read a room, the way disagreement became more opaque without eye contact. Stanton emailed to say he thought the methodology was sound, which was so unusual that I read it three times to make sure I hadn't misunderstood.

## E011 · 2021-09 → 2022-06 · intensity: 7
**Priya's Thesis Finds My Error**

Priya came to me in September with a draft chapter arguing that my 2006 paper was correct about the mechanism but wrong about the timeframe by approximately twelve years. She was right. She'd done the work carefully and the evidence was clear. I read it twice before I told her what I thought, and what I thought was: this is the best gift a doctoral student has ever given me, and also, I've been citing that paper for fifteen years and will now need to revise everything I've cited it in. I told her the first part more easily than the second.

## E012 · 2022-03 → 2022-06 · intensity: 6
**Ukraine, the Economics of Sanctions**

The invasion happened on a Thursday and by Saturday I had three media requests, two email threads with IMF colleagues, and a half-written paper about supply chain inflation. I am an international economist and I have opinions about energy dependency and sanctions architecture that are useful and do not address the fundamental horror of what was happening. I appeared on two television programmes saying accurate things about economic consequences and felt, both times, that accuracy was not quite the right register for the moment. Ravi wrote a climate adaptation piece that week. His was the more honest response.

## E013 · 2023-06 → 2024-03 · intensity: 4
**The Full Professorship, Finally**

I was promoted to full professor eleven years after I should have been, which is not what I said in the department meeting but is what I said to Ravi that evening. The delay was a mixture of gender, the publications record being in heterodox journals, and Stanton's consistent procedural objections over eight years. Ravi opened a bottle of wine we'd been saving. I was genuinely pleased and slightly furious and I know the furious part will fade and the pleased part won't and I'm letting it take its time.

## E014 · 2024-04 → 2024-10 · intensity: 5
**IMF Offer, Second Time**

They offered me a two-year fellowship position in Washington. A proper posting, not consulting. I talked to Ravi about it for three months. He said he could adjust his work geography. My mother called from Chennai and asked why I would leave London. I turned it down and wrote a paper about why the IMF's internal policy formation process is structurally insulated from the research it commissions. The timing was not accidental. I don't think I regret it, and I'm not finished deciding.

## E015 · 2025-08 → 2026-03 · intensity: 6
**The Recession Call, Again**

I published a forecast in August predicting a UK contraction beginning in Q2 2026. It received significant press coverage and two formal challenge papers within six months, one from Stanton's former doctoral student. The challenge is methodologically serious and I'm working on a response. The coverage was gratifying and the challenge is making me more careful than I've been in several years. I've started keeping the notebook again — the one I used to keep at MIT when I was learning to ask the right questions. Maybe that's what getting it wrong publicly does, eventually: it makes you want to earn it.
