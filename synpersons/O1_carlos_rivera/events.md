## E001 · 1997-06 → 1998-04 · intensity: 8
**UNAM Graduation, the First Real Job**

I graduated industrial engineering in June 1997 and took a junior operations role at a mid-size distribution company in Monterrey. The theory was clean in textbooks; the practice was a warehouse with fifty workers, a floor manager who'd been there thirty years, and a routing system that hadn't changed since 1985. I spent eight months watching the gap between the optimal process and the actual process and learning that the gap wasn't ignorance — it was accumulated human decision-making that the textbook didn't account for. The floor manager, Don Ignacio, said: the system works because people make it work, not the other way around. I wrote that down and have been thinking about it ever since.

## E004 · 2008-10 → 2009-08 · intensity: 7
**The Financial Crisis, Mexico**

The 2008 crisis hit Mexican logistics hard in late 2008 — US consumer demand collapsed and with it the cross-border freight volumes that half our clients depended on. I built the cost reduction scenario analysis that saved eight positions that would otherwise have been eliminated, and then watched management cut twelve positions using a different analysis. The twelve who were cut had the lowest seniority. The jobs were gone. The process had been followed. I've spent years thinking about the difference between executing a decision correctly and the decision being correct.

## E006 · 2015-04 → 2016-01 · intensity: 7
**The Org Rebuild, The Credit**

I joined Grupo Meridian Logística in 2014. By spring 2015 I had identified the structural problems: too many reporting layers between operations and decision-making, a performance management system that measured activity rather than outcome, and three regional managers who needed replacement or reorientation. I designed and implemented the rebuild over eight months. Torres presented it at the annual board meeting. The board commended him. He thanked me privately and told me I'd done the best operational work the company had seen. He took credit publicly; I got thanks privately. I've made peace with this. The work is the work.

## E007 · 2019-03 → 2020-01 · intensity: 8
**The Diabetes Diagnosis**

I was diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes at forty-three in March 2019. The A1C was 8.1. My GP was not alarmed; she said it was common at my age and weight and work stress level and entirely manageable. I was alarmed. Isabella drove me home from the appointment and I sat in the kitchen for an hour and she sat with me and did not tell me I'd be fine. She waited for me to say it, which I did eventually, and then she said: yes, but now you're going to have to actually do the things you've been saying you'll do for ten years. I started doing them. ## E008 · 2020-03 → 2020-12 · intensity: 6
**COVID, Supply Chain, Six Months of Problems**

The pandemic produced the operational crisis I'd been building contingency plans for since 2018 and the contingency plans were inadequate. Border closures, health protocol requirements, driver shortages, and demand volatility were simultaneous. I moved into the office for three weeks in March — a cot in the conference room, six hours of sleep, constant calls with regional managers. Torres handled the external and political dimensions and left the operations to me completely, which is exactly the right division of labor between us and also the most demanding six months of my career. By December the network was stable at reduced capacity with fewer staff than we'd started the year with. That math has stayed with me.
