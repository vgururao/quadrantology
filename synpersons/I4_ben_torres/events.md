## E002 · 2009-05 → 2010-03 · intensity: 7
**The Stanford Thesis, the Methodology That Stuck**

My MS thesis developed a statistical framework for identifying early signals of product failure modes in aggregated consumer complaint data. My supervisor said it was technically solid and practically useful, which in engineering academia are not always the same compliment. The framework was specific enough to be patentable and I didn't patent it because I was going to use it in a government context. My father pointed out that this was idealistic. I said I preferred "efficient." The framework has been the methodological backbone of every significant CPSC investigation I've run since. The efficiency was real.

## E003 · 2012-04 → 2013-01 · intensity: 7
**CPSC, the First Investigation**

I joined the CPSC in 2011. My first independent investigation was a defective infant crib component — a side-release buckle that had a failure mode invisible in standard testing and detectable only in the specific thermal conditions that occurred during summer transit in enclosed vehicles. I built the evidence file over eight months: complaint data, material failure analysis, incident reports. Director Huang reviewed it and asked for two additional months of verification. I said the evidence was complete. She said the evidence needed to be unchallengeable before publication. I did two more months of verification. The recall was the largest infant product recall in CPSC history that year. Huang was right about the extra time and I was right about the evidence. Both of those things.

## E008 · 2020-03 → 2021-01 · intensity: 6
**COVID, the Consumer Product Spike**

The pandemic produced a consumer product safety crisis that I found professionally fascinating and personally exhausting. Home gym equipment, home office furniture, children's toys, and air quality devices — all categories that had accelerated into consumer hands without adequate testing and then accelerated into CPSC complaint databases without adequate analysis. I ran four parallel investigations through 2020. Carolina was running three NHTSA investigations simultaneously. We had a household rule about not discussing active cases at dinner that we broke approximately twelve times and enforced approximately eight. The enforcement failures were the more productive evenings.

## E009 · 2021-09 → 2022-06 · intensity: 7
**The Lithium Battery Fire Investigation**

The largest investigation of my CPSC career opened in September 2021: a pattern of residential fires traced to lithium battery packs in aftermarket e-bike conversion kits. Forty-one fires in eighteen months, three fatalities, twelve injuries. The evidence chain from individual incidents to systemic manufacturer failure took nine months to build cleanly. I filed my preliminary finding in June 2022. Director Huang said it was the strongest failure analysis the agency had produced in a decade. The manufacturer disputed and then settled. The recall was voluntary and significant and the settlement included a public safety disclosure that established standards for the category. Carolina heard about it through NHTSA channels before I told her. She called to say the methodology was strong. From her that was significant.

## E010 · 2022-09 → 2023-05 · intensity: 5
**My Father's Paper**

My father published a paper in 2022 arguing that evidence-based regulatory frameworks produce systematically better outcomes than market self-regulation in consumer goods but systematically worse outcomes in services. He sent me a preprint and asked for my assessment of the methodology. I read it three times and wrote back with two methodological comments and one conceptual disagreement. He responded with a four-page rebuttal. We went back and forth for three months and reached a position I'd describe as mutual modification — we each moved slightly and neither of us moved far. The disagreement about the services question remains. I think about it in the context of my own work more than I expected.
